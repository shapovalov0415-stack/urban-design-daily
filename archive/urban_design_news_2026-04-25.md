## New reforms legislated for Victorian apartment buildings

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/new-reforms-legislated-for-victorian-apartment-buildings/  
**Topics:** Melbourne, Australia, Housing, Policy, Zoning

Victoria's new Mid-Rise Code commenced operation on 16 April 2026, introducing deemed-to-comply planning standards for apartment buildings between four and six storeys in designated residential zones. Under the reform, developments that meet state-wide requirements are automatically compliant, with no standing for neighbours to appeal — a significant streamlining of an approval process long criticised as one of the slowest in the country. Clause 57 applies the rules to the Mixed Use Zone, the Township Zone inside Melbourne's urban growth boundary, the Residential Growth Zone, and the new Housing Choice and Transport Zone. The code abolishes the mandatory upper-level setback that produced the so-called "wedding cake" silhouette across inner Melbourne, freeing designers to pursue simpler massing with better thermal performance. It also introduces tree canopy targets of 10–20 per cent on site, alongside new standards for privacy, daylight and communal open space. Heritage and flood overlays continue to operate in parallel, so character precincts are not automatically opened up. Premier Jacinta Allan framed the code as a delivery mechanism for "more homes, faster, in the places people want to live," and the package is pitched to absorb a meaningful share of Melbourne's mid-rise pipeline, where the "missing middle" has historically been trapped between townhouse codes and tower controls. Industry bodies have broadly welcomed the certainty while warning that apartment quality will hinge on how rigorously the landscape and daylight standards are enforced under deemed-to-comply pathways.

**Why it matters:** For urban designers, the Mid-Rise Code reshapes the rulebook for Melbourne's mid-scale streets — trading neighbour-led objection rights for prescribed tree canopy, setback and daylight standards, and putting the onus on designers to make the deemed-to-comply envelope produce genuinely liveable buildings.

## 'Grocery-to-housing' developer proposes 415-unit senior tower pair at Oakland Trader Joe's site

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/04/137427-grocery-housing-developer-wants-bring-415-units-senior-housing-oakland-trader  
**Topics:** US, Housing, Transit, TOD, Policy

San Francisco developer Align has filed an application to replace the Trader Joe's–anchored retail centre at 5727 College Avenue in Oakland's Rockridge neighbourhood with a pair of senior-housing high-rises of 31 and 25 storeys, delivering 415 residences on a 1.5-acre parcel beside the Rockridge BART station. The programme is split between 371 independent-living units, 18 assisted-living units and 26 memory-care units, with an undisclosed non-profit operator lined up to run the facility. Align filed under California's density bonus law and a statute that exempts qualifying housing projects from CEQA environmental review, allowing the scheme to move forward at heights well above the site's base zoning. The firm has previously pursued a similar "grocery-to-housing" model over several Safeway stores in San Francisco, where the grocery component is typically rebuilt at the base of the new towers. Rockridge is different: Albertsons, Safeway's parent, owns the site but is not proposing to replace the Trader Joe's, which is the neighbourhood's only full-service grocer and also the first unionised Trader Joe's in the United States. Community response has been sharply split between housing advocates welcoming dense, transit-adjacent senior homes and residents worried about a food-access desert, the loss of a unionised workforce, and the scale of 31-storey towers dropped into a low-rise commercial strip.

**Why it matters:** The proposal is a stress test for transit-oriented infill under California's density bonus and CEQA exemptions — showing how aggressively those tools can override local scale and land-use expectations, and how "grocery-to-housing" conversions land very differently when the grocery doesn't come back.

## Portland, Maine approved a record number of homes last year — but far fewer got built

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/04/137413-portland-approved-record-number-homes-last-year-far-fewer-got-built  
**Topics:** US, Housing, Policy, Zoning

Portland, Maine's planning board approved 1,420 new housing units in 2025 — a city record and nearly a 200 per cent jump from the 477 units approved the year before — yet only 187 homes were actually added to the city's stock, a 68 per cent drop in completions from 2024. The approval surge was concentrated: a handful of particularly large proposals accounted for roughly 95 per cent of the approved units, meaning most of the pipeline rides on the viability of a few deals rather than a broad base of small and mid-sized projects. Developers have repeatedly cited construction costs, interest rates, and the city's 11-year-old inclusionary zoning rule — which requires 25 per cent of units in new buildings to be deemed affordable, or a per-unit opt-out fee — as the decisive barrier between an approved drawing and a finished building. Portland officials launched a formal review of the IZ policy in December, with findings expected in the coming weeks. Housing advocates are split: some argue the approval record proves the policy still attracts development and should be kept; others, including builders, say the gap between 1,420 approvals and 187 completions is itself the evidence that the numbers no longer pencil. The debate previews a wider reckoning for mid-size US cities balancing affordability mandates against feasibility.

**Why it matters:** The 1,420-versus-187 gap reframes how urban designers should read "housing wins" — permitting data alone masks whether approved projects are actually deliverable, and whether affordability instruments are cross-subsidising homes or quietly stalling them.
