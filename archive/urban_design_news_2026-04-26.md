## Pasadena adopts vision plan for redeveloping the 710 stub

**Source:** Urbanize LA  
**URL:** https://la.urbanize.city/post/pasadena-adopts-vision-plan-redeveloping-710-stub  
**Topics:** Pasadena, Freeway removal, Urban repair, Restorative justice, Housing policy

Pasadena City Council earlier this month adopted the Reconnecting Pasadena vision plan to redevelop approximately 50 acres of land once seized for a never-built 710 Freeway extension. The plan, developed by a consulting team including Perkins Eastman, covers a swath bounded by Union Street, California Boulevard, Pasadena Avenue and St John Avenue. Rather than directly proposing projects, the framework identifies strategies to reconnect the trenched corridor back into the street grid through circulation and development. Key moves include a roundabout at Holly Street to smooth freeway access, converting St. John and Pasadena Avenues from one-way to two-way complete streets, and building a new north-south boulevard along the west side of the trench in place of the existing stub. New east-west connections would extend Valley Street and Bellevue Drive across the corridor. The City Council set a goal of at least 1,800 residential units within the relinquished area. However, the Council excluded restorative justice elements—including targeted affordable housing, business development support and programmes for descendants of those displaced by the original freeway project—from the adopted plan, deferring those provisions for future discussion. The land was seized by Caltrans in the early 1970s, displacing hundreds of residents, primarily Black, Japanese American and Mexican American families. The freeway project was officially killed in 2017 and the corridor relinquished to Pasadena in 2022.

**Why it matters:** The plan demonstrates how cities can reverse mid-century freeway damage through sensitive urban repair, reconnecting severed communities while navigating politically fraught questions about reparative justice and affordable housing obligations after decades of displacement.

## Social architecture as an urban project

**Source:** University of Cincinnati  
**URL:** https://www.uc.edu/news/articles/2026/04/n21404093.html  
**Topics:** Cincinnati, Housing typology, Urban renewal, Public housing, Policy and design

Peter Yi, assistant professor in the University of Cincinnati's school of architecture and interior design, is researching how public policy shapes urban form and who gets to live in cities. Yi's work explores architecture not as isolated objects but as part of a larger social system shaped by policy, history and shared visions. One of his recent projects contributes to a forthcoming Springer book titled 'After Urban Renewal,' edited by Gregory Marinic. Yi's chapter focuses on Cincinnati's West End, where urban renewal displaced established communities and public housing was introduced as both response and oversight mechanism. Yi highlights the evolving—and often overlooked—role of public housing within American cities. Unlike market-driven housing motivated by profit, public housing is funded by the federal government, positioning it as a critical part of the housing landscape with a complicated history. In Cincinnati, Yi examines how the built environment reflects layers of history and changing approaches to housing and urban design. He contrasts inward-turning single-family housing with older, denser typologies that engage more directly with the street, such as the breezeway tenement found in Over-the-Rhine—structures combining ground-floor retail with residences above and side entrances that provide light, air and shared space. Yi argues that policy alone does not produce better cities: designers, architects and planners must study the past and present to use them as springboards for the future.

**Why it matters:** Yi's research reframes architecture as urban policy made visible, reminding practitioners that housing typology, tenure and street engagement are never neutral—they encode decades of political choices about who belongs in the city and how communities are built or displaced.
