## SA and WA rank highest in 2026 planning reform scorecard

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/sa-and-wa-rank-highest-in-2026-planning-reform-scorecard/  
**Topics:** Australia, Planning Policy, Housing, Zoning Reform, Government

The Housing Institute of Australia has released its 2026 Planning Blueprint Scorecard, evaluating states and territories on planning system reforms against the federal government's 2023 National Planning Reform Blueprint. South Australia and Western Australia tied for top position with scores of three out of five, attributed to large-scale rezoning and land release programs, expanded development assessment panels in WA, and digital innovations including a land supply dashboard in SA. Victoria and the ACT tied second with 2.5 out of 5, followed by NSW and Tasmania at 2.0. NSW showed the most progress, advancing from 1.5 in 2025 due to pre-endorsed design pattern books and a new development coordination authority. Disappointingly, no jurisdiction scored higher than three out of five on planning reforms. The HIA called for Commonwealth leadership to provide a 'best practice' toolkit including AI-driven assessment software, supported by planning exemptions and digital portals. The Planning Institute of Australia warned the scorecard risks confusing reform intensity with actual planning system performance and quality outcomes, with CEO Matt Collins noting that 'communities don't experience reform' but rather whether homes get built, how long it takes, and whether infrastructure keeps pace. The HIA also flagged the absence of a federal government-led national dashboard tracking housing data on planning approvals, construction commencements, completion times and delivery constraints.

**Why it matters:** This systematic evaluation of state-level planning reform exposes the gap between policy rhetoric and implementation capacity across Australia, offering urban designers a framework to assess how regulatory change translates into tangible housing outcomes at the metropolitan scale.

## Can revolving loan funds revolutionize housing financing?

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/05/137470-can-revolving-loan-funds-revolutionize-housing-financing  
**Topics:** US, Housing Finance, Policy Innovation, Affordable Housing, Montgomery County

Benjamin Schneider's analysis for Remapping Debate examines the potential of revolving loan funds (RLFs) to unlock a new housing boom through a one-time public investment that cycles indefinitely. The concept originated with Montgomery County, Maryland in 2021, when the county created a revolving loan fund for housing production that policymakers now view as 'a simple, high-impact, and relatively cheap way to stimulate the development of new homes.' Unlike traditional grant programs, RLFs provide capital that is repaid and re-lent, creating a self-sustaining mechanism for housing production. Despite their appeal to many city leaders, the approach has faced political opposition from both ends of the spectrum. In progressive jurisdictions, housing advocates and politicians are wary of programs not directly focused on deeply affordable homes, preferring subsidies targeted at the lowest income brackets. Conversely, despite strong belief in market forces in other economic sectors, many Americans don't see a correlation between housing supply and housing costs, creating political resistance to supply-side interventions. The concept represents a middle ground between purely market-driven development and fully subsidized affordable housing, offering governments a way to leverage limited public funds more efficiently while supporting increased housing production across the affordability spectrum. Its success or failure may influence how municipalities approach housing finance in an era of constrained budgets.

**Why it matters:** Revolving loan funds present an innovative financing mechanism that could fundamentally reshape how cities deploy capital for housing, offering urban designers a scalable tool to unlock stalled projects while maintaining public accountability and long-term fiscal sustainability.

## New framework from Brown University evaluates urban design's impact on mental health

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/05/137479-new-framework-evaluates-urban-design-impact-mental-health  
**Topics:** Mental Health, Urban Design, Research, Public Health, Evidence-Based Planning

Researchers at Brown University have developed a groundbreaking framework to evaluate how urban design elements affect mental health disorders independently of pollution-related factors—representing the first methodology of its kind in the field. The framework marks a significant shift in how planners and designers can assess the psychological impacts of the built environment, moving beyond the traditional focus on air and noise pollution to examine design features such as street configuration, building scale, access to green space, and public realm quality. The new tool allows practitioners to isolate the mental health effects of urban form decisions from environmental contamination, enabling more precise interventions. While the research acknowledges the well-established links between urban density, environmental stressors and mental illness prevalence, the Brown team has created a systematic method for separating design-driven outcomes from pollution-driven ones. The framework is expected to inform evidence-based planning policy, development assessment processes, and urban design guidelines in cities worldwide. By quantifying the relationship between specific design interventions and mental health metrics including depression, anxiety, and cognitive functioning, the tool promises to elevate mental wellbeing as a core consideration alongside physical health, economic vitality, and environmental sustainability in urban planning decisions.

**Why it matters:** This framework gives urban designers an evidence base to advocate for design choices that support mental health—from street trees and human-scaled buildings to walkable neighborhoods—while demonstrating that good urbanism is not just about aesthetics or movement, but measurable public health outcomes.
