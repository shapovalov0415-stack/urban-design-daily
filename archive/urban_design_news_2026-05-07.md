## Planning reform alone won't fix housing supply

**Source:** Council Magazine  
**URL:** https://councilmagazine.com.au/planning-reform-alone-wont-fix-housing-supply  
**Topics:** Australia, Housing, Policy, Planning Reform

<cite index="52-2,52-3">The Planning Institute of Australia (PIA) has welcomed the National Housing Supply and Affordability Council's State of the Housing System 2026 report, released May 1, with PIA Chief Executive Officer Matt Collins noting that despite governments progressing planning reform, affordability continues to worsen and many approved homes are not being built.</cite> <cite index="52-4,52-5">Collins said governments are taking significant action to improve planning systems and enable more housing, including streamlining approvals and supporting greater density, but feasibility and infrastructure issues mean too many approved homes are not progressing to construction, noting that "planning systems are getting better at approving homes, but feasibility and infrastructure issues mean too many approved homes aren't getting built."</cite> The report arrives as Australia pursues the National Housing Accord's target of 1.2 million new homes over five years, but delivery has fallen well short. The PIA response underscores a critical gap between planning policy reform—which state and territory governments have accelerated over 2024–25—and actual construction commencement. While planning bottlenecks are easing, the pipeline is now stalling at the feasibility stage due to construction costs, developer viability concerns, and infrastructure shortfalls. This positions planners as essential coordinators of a multi-sector solution that must integrate financing, building procurement, and long-term infrastructure sequencing to turn approvals into homes on the ground.

**Why it matters:** This highlights a structural disconnect that urban designers must navigate: regulatory reform enables supply on paper, but delivery depends on coordinating infrastructure, finance, and construction feasibility—domains often beyond planning's direct control but essential to housing outcomes.

## Hackensack Meridian Health Opens First-of-Its-Kind Wellness Center at NJ Transit Metropark Station

**Source:** Jersey Digs  
**URL:** https://jerseydigs.com/hackensack-meridian-healths-health-metropark/  
**Topics:** USA, New Jersey, Transit-Oriented Development, Public Health, Infrastructure

<cite index="61-5,57-3">Hackensack Meridian Health's Health & Wellness Center at Metropark officially opened April 29, 2026, next to Metropark, New Jersey Transit's second-busiest train station, offering patients extended hours from 6 a.m. to 8:30 p.m. on weekdays to pop in for appointments before or after commuting.</cite> <cite index="62-6">The $200 million center includes advanced imaging, urgent care, primary care, surgical specialties, medical specialties, retail pharmacy, physical therapy, occupational therapy, rehabilitation, and phlebotomy.</cite> <cite index="57-6,57-7">The development is the first example of New Jersey Transit's broader LAND Plan (Leveraging Assets for Non-farebox Dollars), announced in October 2025, with NJ TRANSIT President & CEO Kris Kolluri describing it as "a model for what's possible under public private partnerships."</cite> <cite index="60-2,60-13">The larger Metropark redevelopment by DOR—a consortium led by Russo Development awarded a $110 million tax credit—will add approximately 230 residential units with ground-floor retail.</cite> <cite index="61-10">Hackensack Meridian Health is also consolidating its corporate headquarters into the building, uniting employees across several locations for the first time since the system's merger ten years ago.</cite> <cite index="62-15">New Jersey Governor Mikie Sherrill framed it as transit-oriented development that "makes it easier for residents to live, work, and access care without relying on a car, strengthening the connection between healthy communities and sustainable infrastructure."</cite>

**Why it matters:** This represents a new frontier for transit-oriented development: leveraging underutilized transit agency land for non-residential civic uses that anchor mixed-use precincts and generate non-farebox revenue, demonstrating how TOD principles extend beyond housing to support equitable access to essential services.
