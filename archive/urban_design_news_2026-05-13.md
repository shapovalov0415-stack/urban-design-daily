## Trump's Transportation Secretary Announces $28.5 Million Investment to Make Mass Transit More Family Friendly

**Source:** Federal Transit Administration  
**URL:** https://www.transit.dot.gov/about/news/trumps-transportation-secretary-sean-p-duffy-announces-285-million-investment-make-mass  
**Topics:** TOD, United States, Policy, Transit, Federal Funding

On May 11, 2026, U.S. Transportation Secretary Sean P. Duffy announced a $28.5 million Notice of Funding Opportunity through the Federal Transit Administration's Pilot Program for Transit-Oriented Development Planning. The revamped program shifts federal TOD priorities toward safety, innovation, and family-centered design, with applications due July 10, 2026. The NOFO prioritizes three key criteria: safety improvements along transit corridors and at stations, particularly routes to schools, childcare, and recreation; innovative approaches and public-private partnerships to deliver TOD infrastructure; and increasing transit access for families, including integrating childcare and recreation near public transportation hubs. Secretary Duffy framed the initiative as part of President Trump's agenda to make commuting easier and more affordable for families, stating the program will invest in corridors that connect families to jobs, education, and healthcare. The funding represents a notable policy reorientation for the federal TOD program, which previously emphasized density, housing supply, and climate goals. The new priorities signal federal interest in leveraging transit planning for social infrastructure and family services, rather than solely housing production or emissions reduction.

**Why it matters:** This federal policy pivot toward family-oriented TOD introduces new evaluation criteria that urban designers and transit planners must now address—childcare proximity, recreation access, and safe routes for children—potentially reshaping station-area plans and TOD feasibility studies across the U.S.

## Above the Tracks: China's Unique Model for Transit-Oriented Development

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/05/above-tracks-chinas-unique-model-transit-oriented-development  
**Topics:** TOD, China, Housing, Transit, International

Published May 8, 2026, Anderson Chider Hsieh examines China's distinctive approach to transit-oriented development, where cities since 2010 have increasingly built mixed-use neighborhoods directly above metro depots and rail yards. The model leverages large tracts of state-owned land near transit infrastructure to create dense, integrated communities atop operational rail facilities. Hsieh's analysis, drawing on examples from Shenzhen and other Chinese cities, highlights how this vertical integration maximizes land value and transit access while maintaining depot functionality below. The approach contrasts sharply with Western TOD models, which typically redevelop adjacent parcels but leave depot sites untouched due to fragmented land ownership, operational constraints, and regulatory barriers. Hsieh argues that U.S. transit agencies could reevaluate the redevelopment potential of their extensive rail yards and depot holdings, which often occupy prime, transit-accessible land in inner-city locations. The article suggests that air-rights development and deck-over strategies could unlock significant housing and mixed-use capacity, though institutional barriers—including federal restrictions on non-transit revenue generation and local zoning—would need reform. The piece positions depot-top development as an untapped frontier for North American TOD, particularly as housing crises intensify pressure to maximize land near existing infrastructure.

**Why it matters:** China's depot-top urbanism offers a provocative model for Western cities facing acute land scarcity near transit—challenging planners to rethink rail yards not as off-limits operational zones but as development platforms that could yield thousands of homes on publicly owned, transit-rich sites.

## The Australian Housing Landscape as of March 2026

**Source:** BDO Australia  
**URL:** https://www.bdo.com.au/en-au/insights/real-estate-construction/the-australian-housing-landscape-as-of-march-2026  
**Topics:** Australia, New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, Housing, Policy, Zoning

BDO's inaugural housing market pulse, published in late April 2026, provides a state-by-state analysis of Australia's housing sector as of March 2026, examining policy reforms, market conditions, and delivery challenges. New South Wales emerges as the most ambitious reformer, with state-led rezonings, expanded complying development pathways, pre-endorsed design pattern books, and the creation of a development coordination authority all signaling serious attempts to tackle pipeline delays. The reforms emphasize higher-density supply in established locations and around transport corridors, with faster approvals and streamlined development pathways designed to support infill housing. However, BDO notes that feasibility remains challenging due to elevated construction costs, tight contracting capacity, and prolonged approval and mobilization timeframes, which squeeze margins through escalation and holding costs. Victoria entered 2026 with subdued conditions as buyers remain price-sensitive and activity concentrates in more affordable segments. Queensland remained one of Australia's best-performing markets in 2025, with Brisbane house prices increasing approximately 9 percent over the year and regional markets recording 13-14 percent growth driven by lifestyle demand and interstate migration, though signs of moderation emerged in late 2025 as affordability tightened. Unit rents stabilized in late 2025, suggesting affordability pressures may be tempering demand. The report emphasizes that long-term delivery will require sustained collaboration, infrastructure investment, and ongoing policy refinement, with stronger tenancy laws expected to enhance renter stability though investor sentiment may remain cautious.

**Why it matters:** NSW's comprehensive planning reforms—combining rezoning, pattern books, and a new delivery authority—represent Australia's most assertive institutional response to housing undersupply, offering a potential blueprint (or cautionary tale) for other states grappling with the National Housing Accord's 1.2 million dwelling target.
