## A Lesson From Urban Icons on Designing Public Space: Get Rid of Traffic

**Source:** CityLab (Bloomberg)  
**URL:** https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/anne-hidalgo-and-norman-foster-have-lessons-on-designing-public-space  
**Topics:** Public Realm, Madrid, Paris, Traffic, Urban Design

At a closed-door mayoral forum during the Bloomberg CityLab conference in Madrid on April 27, 2026, former Paris Mayor Anne Hidalgo and British architect Norman Foster shared success stories about using design to improve environmental and economic outcomes in cities. Hidalgo, who was born in Cádiz, Spain, discussed three strategies she employed during her tenure, including her work to make the Seine clean enough to swim—a project inspired by her childhood love of swimming on Spain's oceanside beaches. The forum, organized by Bloomberg Philanthropies and the Aspen Institute, brought together dozens of mayors in Madrid to discuss city transformation. Both urban visionaries talked about a shared strategy behind their successful visions: reducing traffic to create better public spaces. The conference, held April 27-29, 2026, convened more than 700 mayors, policymakers, innovators, and creatives to address major issues facing cities worldwide including AI, housing and affordability, and the role of cities as bulwarks of democracy in an era of polarization. The 12th Bloomberg CityLab focused on strengthening city-to-city connections and confronting urgent shared challenges. Featured speakers included Michael R. Bloomberg, Nadia Calviño (President of the European Investment Bank), Norman Foster, and José Luis Martínez-Almeida (Mayor of Madrid). More than 100 city leaders attended the Mayors Innovation Studio on April 27, which challenged mayors to rethink design as one of the most powerful tools of city leadership.

**Why it matters:** The convergence of political and design leadership around traffic reduction as a core strategy for better public space demonstrates a maturing global consensus on urban design priorities, with direct lessons from two of Europe's most influential urban figures.

## Transit-oriented development simply makes sense

**Source:** St Pete Catalyst  
**URL:** https://stpetecatalyst.com/transit-oriented-development-simply-makes-sense/  
**Topics:** St Petersburg, Transit-Oriented Development, BRT, Zoning Reform, Affordable Housing

St. Petersburg, Florida is advancing higher-density land use regulations around PSTA's SunRunner bus rapid transit stations to encourage transit-oriented development (TOD). The city is creating optional, not mandatory, TOD Overlay regulations for developers, who can choose to build with existing land use stipulations or opt into proposed SunRunner TOD options that facilitate pedestrian- and transit-oriented development rather than car-centric design. These higher-density areas focus on the SunRunner corridor running between downtown and the beaches along 1st Avenue N and 1st Avenue S, with larger higher-density zones around SunRunner stops. The plan uses density bonuses, workforce housing bonus provisions, and eliminates parking requirements to incentivize developers to include affordable units. The SunRunner TEC (Target Employment Center) overlay was previously adopted in 2024 along 22nd Street S leading to historically Black neighborhoods. Larry Silvestri, writing for the Catalyst's Community Voices platform, argues that based on the experience of other cities, TOD can have significant positive community impact over time, with new development at reasonable specified heights creating new activity nodes west of downtown tied to the premium transit line that has proven successful since launching in 2022. The regulations are designed to encourage affordable housing options through reduced or eliminated parking minimums that reflect actual transit-adjacent need rather than conventional requirements.

**Why it matters:** This story illustrates how mid-sized American cities are adapting zoning frameworks to capture the value of BRT investments, using optional overlays and parking reform to shift from auto-dependent sprawl toward compact, mixed-use districts—a live laboratory for TOD implementation that Melbourne practitioners can benchmark against their own tram and metro catchments.

## Beyond the Street: Climate, Commerce, and the Evolution of Hong Kong's Elevated Networks

**Source:** ArchDaily  
**URL:** https://www.archdaily.com/1040682/beyond-the-street-climate-commerce-and-the-evolution-of-hong-kongs-elevated-networks  
**Topics:** Hong Kong, Public Realm, Elevated Networks, Urban Design, Asia

Fourteen years after the publication of *Cities Without Ground: A Hong Kong Guidebook*, ArchDaily revisits Hong Kong's elevated pedestrian networks to assess how the city's tiered urbanism has evolved. The original 2012 book documented Hong Kong's dependence on second-storey pedestrian systems that separate people from traffic, extend commercial frontage beyond ground level, and negotiate hilly topography where flat circulation is an engineered achievement. Since then, these elevated systems have grown in prominence, recasting public space as continuous yet selective, connective yet curated. The recently opened East Coast Promenade exemplifies this evolution, stretching along the northern edge of Hong Kong Island as part of a 13-kilometer continuous harbourfront walking network. Rather than simply moving people efficiently above vehicular infrastructure, the promenade piggybacks beneath the Island Eastern Corridor highway to create an additional pedestrian layer with intentional pauses, overlooks, and community-oriented pockets. This infrastructural gesture shifts the typical elevated network strategy away from traffic separation toward waterfront engagement, acknowledging how Hong Kong residents have historically claimed elevated spaces as places to linger and gather. The article raises critical questions about whether Hong Kong's expanded elevated networks have learned to engage the street more deliberately through stairs, ramps, and public rooms, or whether they continue hovering as a parallel world, examining what it now means to live and design within a metropolis increasingly organized by vertical levels.

**Why it matters:** Hong Kong's vertical public realm offers crucial lessons for high-density Asian cities (and Melbourne's CBD laneways) on how elevated networks can balance pedestrian connectivity with street-level vitality, climate protection, and genuine public access—not just privatized through-routes—particularly as cities worldwide grapple with climate adaptation and intensifying urban cores.
