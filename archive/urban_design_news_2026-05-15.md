## FTA announces $28.5 million in funding for Transit-Oriented Development Planning

**Source:** Mass Transit / Federal Transit Administration  
**URL:** https://www.masstransitmag.com/management/news/55376986/us-department-of-transportation-dot-usdot-issues-285-million-nofo-for-grants-through-pilot-program-for-tod-planning  
**Topics:** United States, TOD, Policy, Federal Funding, Transit

The Federal Transit Administration (FTA) announces the opportunity to apply for $28,492,618 in competitive grants for the Fiscal Year (FY) 2026 Pilot Program for Transit-Oriented Development Planning (TOD Pilot Program). As required by Federal public transportation law, Section 20005(b) of the Moving Ahead for Progress in the 21st Century Act (MAP–21), as amended by section 30009 of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, and subject to funding availability, funds will be awarded competitively to support comprehensive or site-specific planning associated with new fixed guideway and core capacity improvement transit projects. USDOT says it is looking to partner with communities with proposals that advance safety, innovation and make commuting easier. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy stated the revamped program will invest in transit corridors that connect families to jobs, education and healthcare, which can improve regional mobility. Evaluation criteria include innovation—how a project fosters innovative approaches, partnerships with transit agencies and advocates and promotes research, innovation and demonstration of new technologies and policies that stimulate private sector participation—and benefits for families and communities, including how a project increases transit access for families at the proposed transit station or along the corridor and how it integrates childcare and recreation near public transportation hubs. Project proposals must be submitted electronically through Grants.gov by 11:59 p.m. Eastern time on July 10, 2026.

**Why it matters:** The program signals renewed federal commitment to land-use/transport integration in the U.S., with a notable emphasis on family amenities and innovation—criteria that could reshape how planners pitch TOD projects and what gets built around stations.

## How cities are turning urban complexity into coherent climate plans

**Source:** World Economic Forum  
**URL:** https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/04/cities-climate-action-plans/  
**Topics:** Climate Adaptation, Infrastructure, Policy, EBRD, Green Cities

Cities are crucial to the climate transition – their buildings, energy systems, transportation and urban design offer interconnected opportunities to decarbonize. To secure investment in climate-related projects, cities must establish a vision, deploy systems thinking and collaborate across public and private sectors. Green City Action Plans, delivered as part of EBRD Green Cities, offer a framework for connecting environmental challenges with sustainable infrastructure investments and policy measures. Once identified, these projects need to be prepared for investment. If a city is targeting investment from a multilateral development bank (MDB), this requires a feasibility study and concept design. Does the new fleet of electric buses or the wastewater treatment plant make sense? What should the technical specifications be? To ensure a project is bankable, financiers must be involved from the outset. Infrastructure requires sizable investments, and it takes time to build trust. If MDBs are to invest, they will need to ensure that, right from the feasibility stage, a project meets their requirements. The European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD)'s Green Cities programme fosters this kind of collaborative, long-term relationship with over 50 cities. The Green Cities programme identifies, prioritizes and connects cities' environmental challenges with sustainable infrastructure investments and policy measures. The article emphasises that climate action in cities is not simply a technical challenge but requires aligning vision, systems thinking, and cross-sector collaboration to make projects bankable and implementable at scale.

**Why it matters:** This operationalises the 'climate adaptation masterplan' conversation by showing how cities can move from aspiration to bankable infrastructure projects, a gap many municipalities struggle to bridge when translating urban design ambitions into funded works.

## Kerstin Thompson Architects and Nelson and Koo unveil design for 3.6-hectare Adelaide complex

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/kerstin-thompson-architects-unveils-design-for-36-hectare-adelaide-complex/  
**Topics:** Australia, Adelaide, South Australia, Urban Regeneration, Mixed-Use Development, Sustainability

Melbourne-based Kerstin Thompson Architects (KTA) and Nelson and Koo have unveiled designs for a $250 million mixed-use development in Forestville, Adelaide's inner south-west, on a 3.6-hectare former industrial site. Developed in collaboration with Commercial Retail Group and Renewal SA, the project involves a multidisciplinary team including RN Architecture, Studio Gram, Designinc, Matter Engineering, Lucid Consulting, Future Urban and Ellara. The architectural massing consists of three distinct forms: a mid-rise short-stay accommodation building fronting Anzac Highway, two apartment buildings in the centre, and a collection of terraces along the eastern boundary, unified by consistent materiality and datum lines. The scheme is anchored in principles drawn from global 'blue zones'—geographic regions identified by researchers as places of human longevity and wellbeing—embedding opportunities for social connection and walkability. At its centre, the development includes a market hall 'agora' and two external public realms conceived as 'plateia' (Greek communal meeting places). The sustainability-focused program includes an 'urban green school' and a 3,600-square-metre productive rooftop farm. Construction is anticipated to begin in late 2026, positioning the project as what KTA describes as 'a catalyst for urban regeneration' that supports daily life through thoughtful design and diverse community amenities.

**Why it matters:** This project demonstrates how large-scale adaptive reuse of former industrial sites can integrate mixed-use density with blue zone wellbeing principles, productive urban agriculture, and public realm innovation—offering a replicable model for inner-urban regeneration in Australian cities.
