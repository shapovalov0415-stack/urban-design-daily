## Winners revealed: Australian Urban Design Awards 2026

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/winners-revealed-2026-australian-urban-design-awards/  
**Topics:** Melbourne, Australia, Public Space, Heritage

The 2026 Australian Urban Design Awards have named twelve winners — nine projects drawn from a 27-strong shortlist plus three individuals — across four categories: Built Outcomes, Research and Advocacy, Strategic Design and Policy, and Urban Design Champions. Over 80 entries came in this cycle. The Built Outcomes category produced three joint winners that read, together, as a program statement about what Australian urban design is prizing in 2026. Balam Balam Place in Brunswick, Melbourne, by Kennedy Nolan with Open Work and Finding Infinity, was commended as a socially inclusive civic precinct that renews existing heritage fabric rather than replacing it. The St Kilda Pier Redevelopment by Jackson Clements Burrows Architects, Site Office Landscape Architecture and AW Maritime was described by the jury as a major piece of civic engineering that is both resilient and whimsical, and a vital component of enhanced public realm and consolidated coastal infrastructure responding to decades of master planning along the foreshore. The third co-winner, Hill Thalis Architecture and Urban Projects' Campbelltown Station Commuter Carpark, reconceives a utilitarian typology as civic infrastructure — not simply an infrastructure for cars, but a resilient, civic-minded and genuinely optimistic car park, in the practice's own framing. The jury — Matt Pullinger, Bill Tsakalos, Leigh Woolley and Emily Wong — read the wider field as a gentler, Indigenous- and community-led register: ecological landscapes, heritage continuity, and everyday gathering rather than signature architecture. Two Melbourne projects in the top tier signal the city's growing capacity to treat piers, laneways and heritage precincts as civic infrastructure in their own right.

*Why it matters for an urban designer:* Two of three Built Outcomes winners sit in Melbourne — a useful brief for how juries now read heritage reuse and waterfront civic engineering.

## Coldefy Leads Winning Masterplan to Transform Budapest Brownfield into Rewilded Urban District

**Source:** ArchDaily  
**URL:** https://www.archdaily.com/1040337/coldefy-leads-winning-masterplan-to-transform-budapest-brownfield-into-rewilded-urban-district  
**Topics:** Europe, Public Space, Transit, Climate

A team led by French practice Coldefy — with CITYFÖRSTER, Sporaarchitects, TREIBHAUS.LAND and Marko & Placemakers — has won Budapest's largest urban competition, the Rákosrendező Masterplan, to redevelop a long-neglected railway brownfield on the eastern side of the Hungarian capital. The 15-year scheme targets over 10,000 apartments plus commercial, civic and cultural uses, distributed across six sub-districts linked by a continuous loop of public space, and structured around 15-minute city principles so that daily needs sit within walking distance of home. Landscape is the masterplan's backbone: a 15-hectare Forest Park connects across the existing rail line to a new Railway Park, with meadows, a central lake, event spaces, and an interwoven blue-and-green infrastructure network designed to carry stormwater, cool summer temperatures and seed biodiversity back into what the city has long called its rust belt. The existing Rákosrendező station is reimagined as a multimodal hub anchoring the central urban district and plugging into an extended metro line, a new tram line and a network of active mobility routes, which in turn supports largely car-free residential streets and squares. Former railway depots and buildings are slated for adaptive reuse as a cultural centre housing a municipal library and a railway museum. The competition jury singled out the proposal for a livable residential environment and the exceptional quality of its public space system. Ecological continuity, not density alone, reads as the winning move, and the site's rail heritage is treated as civic patrimony rather than an obstacle to remediation.

*Why it matters for an urban designer:* A rare large-scale European masterplan where rewilded landscape, not the block plan, is the structuring idea — a precedent worth filing for Fishermans Bend.

## Maryland unveils plan for 5,000 homes near transit

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/04/137321-maryland-unveils-plan-5000-homes-near-transit  
**Topics:** US, Housing, Transit, Policy

Maryland Governor Wes Moore has unveiled the Baltimore Regional Transit-Oriented Development Strategy, a state-led plan to convert 134 acres of state-owned land — most of it surface parking at or beside transit stations — into roughly 5,000 new homes, and to generate an estimated US$1.4 billion in combined state and local tax revenue. Of the 134 acres, 46 acres sit in Baltimore City, 66 in Baltimore County and 23 in Anne Arundel County, giving the strategy a regional footprint rather than a city-only one. The document is framed as a joint-development playbook for state agencies, local governments and private partners, with station-by-station briefs covering market readiness, zoning and available land, plus financing tools including tax increment financing, enterprise and opportunity-zone incentives, and a strategic loan fund administered by Maryland's economic development corporation. The first test site is the Rogers Avenue Metro Station, where the state is already seeking a development partner for roughly nine acres that could support up to 400 homes and generate about US$27 million in tax revenue. Moore paired the land strategy with a transit-service commitment — 78 new rail cars by 2027 — signalling that rebuilding ridership and rebuilding demand for station-adjacent housing are being treated as a single policy. For urban designers the strategy is notable less for its unit targets than for the land it identifies: parking lots at existing metro, light rail and MARC stations, framed not as infrastructure but as an underused land bank. It is a state-level answer to the familiar American problem that TOD parcels usually sit in municipal hands with mismatched zoning.

*Why it matters for an urban designer:* State-owned station parking reframed as a housing land bank — a play Victoria could run at PTV car parks alongside the Suburban Rail Loop.
