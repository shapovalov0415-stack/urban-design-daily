# Urban Design News — 2026-04-20

## 1. NSW government proposes single statewide community consultation framework
**Source:** ArchitectureAU
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/NSW-government-releases-draft-plan-to-introduce-single-statewide-consultation-framework/

The NSW government has released a draft statewide Community Participation Plan that would replace more than 100 separate local plans with a single, consistent process for how the public is consulted on planning proposals across the state. The draft, released on 8 April by the Minns Labor government, addresses what the planning department describes as "inconsistent consultation practices and confusion" — a patchwork where timeframes, notification thresholds and exhibition requirements vary between councils, leaving residents, practitioners and applicants unsure how to engage. Under the draft, minimum consultation periods for significant state-level strategic planning initiatives extend from 45 to 60 days, reflecting an argument that strategic plans — the instruments that actually shape precincts and metropolitan structure — deserve more scrutiny, not less. Notification of complying development is standardised with a mandatory seven-day notice to neighbours before works begin. In a deliberate trade-off, councils will no longer need to publicly exhibit low-impact rules-compliant applications like houses on compliant lots, sheds and pools, speeding up assessment by removing exhibition overhead on applications where there is nothing meaningful to consult on. The draft preserves councils' ability to adapt their own engagement strategies to local circumstances, meaning place-specific processes can still sit over the top of the standardised base layer. Peak development bodies have welcomed the move as reducing the overhead of operating across multiple LGAs; local government associations have pushed back on aspects that narrow council discretion. The draft and accompanying discussion paper are on public exhibition until 3 June 2026 through the NSW Planning Portal, with submissions open to individuals, councils, industry bodies and community groups.

**Why it matters:** Consultation frameworks are urban design infrastructure. Who gets consulted on which strategic plans shapes what precincts become — a live precedent as Plan Melbourne's next refresh looms.

## 2. Maryland unveils plan for 5,000 homes near Baltimore transit stations
**Source:** Planetizen
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/04/137321-maryland-unveils-plan-5000-homes-near-transit

Maryland Governor Wes Moore and Baltimore Mayor Brandon Scott have unveiled the Baltimore Regional Transit-Oriented Development Strategy, a plan to activate 134 acres of state-owned land near Baltimore metro and light rail stations for roughly 5,000 new homes. The sites — most currently operating as surface parking lots spread across 17 stations in Baltimore City and Anne Arundel and Baltimore counties — are explicitly targeted because they are already publicly owned, already adjacent to high-capacity transit, and already producing minimal public value as asphalt. The state projects about 1.4 billion dollars in added state and local tax revenue over the life of the program. The first site moving to market is roughly nine acres surrounding the Rogers Avenue Metro Station in northwest Baltimore, where the state is seeking a development partner to deliver up to 400 homes, projected to generate about 27 million dollars in state and city tax revenue from that parcel alone. Mayor Scott framed the parking-lot-to-housing swap explicitly in urban design terms: reducing the need for people to drive to the station weakens the case for large park-and-ride footprints and lets the station precinct be rebuilt as a walkable neighbourhood. Moore paired the announcement with broader transit investment, including 78 new rail cars scheduled by 2027, and flagged a Maryland Transit and Housing Opportunity Act that would unlock more than 300 acres of state-owned land adjacent to existing transit stations. For designers, the precedent is the combination of an enabling law, a pre-identified portfolio of state-owned land, and a concrete first pipeline project released simultaneously — a delivery package rather than aspirational policy.

**Why it matters:** Surface parking at transit stations is latent public land. Releasing it as a packaged pipeline, not site-by-site, is the delivery pattern Plan Melbourne's activity centres need.

## 3. Elevated Infrastructure and Public Space: Reclaiming the Ground Below
**Source:** ArchDaily
**URL:** https://www.archdaily.com/1040374/elevated-infrastructure-and-public-space-reclaiming-the-ground-below

ArchDaily's latest editorial essay examines how cities are reclaiming the ambiguous spaces beneath elevated infrastructure — highway flyovers, metro viaducts, rail bridges — as intentional public realm rather than as leftover shade. The piece's framing is structural: elevation reorganises the city vertically, letting speed, capital and visibility flow above while producing a shaded, fragmented second ground below that rarely receives the same design attention as the structures above. Where that second ground is neglected, it becomes the residue of infrastructure — unprogrammed, often hostile, and culturally coded as abandoned. Where it is designed with the rigour of a plaza or park, it becomes a continuous civic landscape. The article draws on case studies including the Bentway Staging Grounds in Toronto, where the undercroft of the Gardiner Expressway has been reworked through lighting, flexible ground treatments and seasonal programming into a programmable public stage; Miyashita Park in Tokyo, an early prototype of re-engineering elevated rail fabric as park-over-rail; and the Taichung Green Corridor in Taiwan, where a former railway alignment has been rebuilt as a continuous public spine integrating landscape, mobility and social infrastructure. The recurring design lesson the piece distils is that reclaiming under-infrastructure space demands continuity — of lighting, of ground plane, of program — rather than site-by-site intervention. It also argues these spaces are uniquely valuable as climate infrastructure: already shaded, already cool, already connected to transit. For dense cities running out of available ground for parks, the space under viaducts reads as the last accessible reserve of civic space, and the precedents are now mature enough to adapt rather than reinvent.

**Why it matters:** Melbourne's West Gate and CityLink edges are the local equivalent — underused civic reserve in plain sight. Continuity of ground plane is the design move to steal.
