## Commonwealth Bank Downgrades Housing Price Forecasts Following Federal Budget Tax Changes

**Source:** Commonwealth Bank  
**URL:** https://www.commbank.com.au/articles/newsroom/2026/05/2026-budget-updated-housing-outlook.html  
**Topics:** Australia, Housing, Policy, Finance, Negative Gearing

Commonwealth Bank has revised its national dwelling price growth forecast to 3 percent for the year ending December 2026, down from a previous projection of 5 percent, following the Federal Government's May 2026 Budget announcement of sweeping changes to residential property investment taxation. The Budget package centers on two major reforms: limiting negative gearing for residential property to new builds only, and replacing the 50 percent capital gains tax discount with cost base indexation and a 30 percent minimum tax rate. CBA economists estimate these changes will reduce house prices by approximately 3 percent below what they otherwise would have been, with smaller impacts on rents. The analysis suggests the removal of negative gearing is equivalent to a 90-155 basis point increase in investor mortgage rates in immediate cash-flow terms for highly leveraged investors with high marginal tax rates and low rental yields. However, the Budget design allows quarantined losses to be carried forward against future residential rental income or capital gains. The bank notes that three cash rate hikes earlier in 2026, taking the rate to 3.85 percent, have already subtracted 1.5 percentage points from price growth forecasts, and the ongoing Middle East conflict has increased construction costs and created supply uncertainty. The Budget also includes a $2 billion Local Infrastructure Fund, extended bans on foreign purchases of established dwellings, and strengthened affordability requirements for build-to-rent tax concessions.

**Why it matters:** This analysis quantifies how federal tax policy shifts directly impact housing affordability and investment viability, giving urban designers hard data on the interplay between fiscal settings, construction finance, and dwelling supply trajectories—critical inputs for feasibility modeling and precinct planning.

## To Improve Transit Service, We Need Better Transit-Oriented Development: The Case for Build HUBS

**Source:** Transportation For America  
**URL:** https://t4america.org/2026/05/13/to-improve-transit-service-we-need-better-transit-oriented-development/  
**Topics:** United States, TOD, Transit, Housing, Ridership

Transportation For America published an analysis on May 13, 2026, arguing that maximizing existing transit investments requires far more transit-oriented development to boost ridership and financial sustainability. The article highlights how low ridership traps transit systems in a "horrid cycle" where reduced fare revenue leads to service cuts, further undermining reliability. The proposed Build Housing Unlock Benefits and Services (Build HUBS) Act targets reforms to the Railroad Rehabilitation and Financing (RRIF) and Transportation Infrastructure Finance and Innovation Act (TIFIA) programs to better support TOD and housing near transit. The piece cites Chicago's 2013 Wicker Park development—a 11-story building with 99 mixed-income apartments, office space, and ground-floor retail across from the Division Blue Line station—as a catalyst for broader neighborhood transformation and economic growth. It also profiles Hennepin County, Minnesota's 2015 light rail TOD project at Lake Street station, which delivered 500 housing units, retail space, and a Human Services Center, significantly improving access to services for residents. The former Metro Transit General Manager is quoted emphasizing that "transit-oriented development is about letting people choose where to live, work and recreate—and giving them the freedom to make those choices without worrying about the financial burdens they'll face when it comes to transportation." The article underscores that TOD strategies—building commercial and residential spaces in walkable proximity to high-quality transit—are essential to breaking the low-ridership feedback loop.

**Why it matters:** The piece connects transit viability directly to land use intensity, making the case that station-area densification isn't cosmetic but existential for service frequency and network resilience—a strategic rationale Melbourne designers can deploy when advocating for higher FSRs and mixed-use zoning around metro stations.
