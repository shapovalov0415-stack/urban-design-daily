## Five simple zoning changes that any city can make to increase housing affordability

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/features/137355-five-simple-zoning-changes-any-city-can-make-increase-housing-affordability  
**Topics:** Zoning Reform, Housing Policy, Affordability, Regulatory Change, North America

Planning attorney Donald Elliott, author of 'An Even Better Way to Zone,' argues that zoning reform need not be rocket science and proposes five practical changes to dramatically boost housing supply. First, allow medium-density housing in commercial zones—an opportunity created by COVID-19's legacy of vacant strip shopping centers along arterials. Second, permit housing on institutional properties like churches and schools. Third, allow small-scale multifamily infill on large single-family lots. Fourth, eliminate arbitrary residential density limits (maximum dwelling units per acre or minimum lot area per unit) that function as 'belt and suspenders' regulations alongside existing height and setback standards, perversely reducing housing affordability. Fifth, legalize microapartments and co-living arrangements. Elliott emphasizes these are mechanically simple amendments—just updating permitted-use tables in zone districts—but notes greater supply alone may not bring prices down, though no long-term affordability solution exists without significantly increased supply. Each reform targets regulatory constraints within local government control, offering a concrete roadmap for communities seeking housing solutions without wholesale zoning code rewrites.

**Why it matters:** This provides a pragmatic toolkit for practitioners navigating the housing crisis, translating abstract zoning-reform rhetoric into specific, actionable code amendments that can pass councils and survive legal challenge while addressing affordability through supply-side liberalization.

## How Cities Design Public Life in the Shade

**Source:** ArchDaily  
**URL:** https://www.archdaily.com/1038054/how-cities-design-public-life-in-the-shade  
**Topics:** Climate Adaptation, Public Space, Urban Heat, Singapore, Spain

As urban heat becomes an everyday condition rather than an exceptional event, ArchDaily explores how cities are treating shade as civic infrastructure requiring long-term governance, maintenance cycles, and embedded care. The piece examines diverse strategies: Singapore's freestanding metal canopies and covered walkways extending continuous shade networks between transit stops; Spanish cities alternating between seasonal fabric canopies strung along pedestrian corridors and freestanding plaza structures that create temporary urban rooms; and purpose-designed shade elements integrated into streetscapes and public squares. The article emphasizes that the design problem isn't simply knowing where shade exists but ensuring strategic distribution—placing shade where people walk, wait, gather, and linger with enough continuity to shape real behavior rather than isolated comfort pockets. Critically, adequacy depends on governance: permitting constraints, irrigation access, liability frameworks, street design standards, and maintenance protocols determine what gets built, how long it lasts, and whether it remains functional. A canopy without upkeep becomes a hazard; a tree without adequate soil volume becomes a failed promise. The piece positions shade infrastructure as requiring the same spatial thinking planners apply to transit networks or green corridors—hierarchy, rhythm, purpose, and systemic integration.

**Why it matters:** This shifts shade from amenity to essential urban infrastructure, equipping designers with spatial frameworks and governance models to integrate climate adaptation into the everyday public realm as extreme heat reshapes how cities function year-round.
