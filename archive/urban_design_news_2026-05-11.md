## Brisbane Reveals Density Shift to Unlock 6000 Homes

**Source:** The Urban Developer  
**URL:** https://www.theurbandeveloper.com/articles/brisbane-low-medium-density-reforms-proposed-queensland  
**Topics:** Australia, Brisbane, Queensland, Housing, Zoning, Transit-Oriented Development

Brisbane City Council has proposed significant planning reforms targeting low-to-medium density residential (LMR) zones as part of its 'More Homes, Sooner' initiative, which could unlock approximately 6,000 new homes across 14 percent of Brisbane's residential areas. The reforms, announced following a May 2026 review by Mayor Adrian Schrinner, would increase allowable building heights from 2-3 storeys to a baseline of 3 storeys, with up to 4 storeys permitted near frequent public transport or retail centres. Minimum lot sizes would drop dramatically from 260 square metres to 120 square metres, making townhouses, row houses, units and small apartments more viable in suburbs including Morningside, St Lucia, Moorooka and Zillmere. The reforms also simplify development assessment processes and expand subdivision eligibility from 200 metres to 300 metres from shopping centres. The initiative responds to a sharp decline in LMR zone development, which has fallen from around 1,100 homes annually to just 445 in 2023 as rising construction costs have made smaller projects commercially challenging. City Planning chair Adam Allan noted that the reforms would empower private developers, who deliver 96 percent of Brisbane's homes, to respond to demand from more than 600 people moving to the city each week. The proposal follows similar density reforms in Sydney, where the NSW Government's Low and Mid-Rise Housing Policy opened low-density zones across metropolitan areas to dual occupancies, terraces, townhouses and small apartments within 800 metres of transit or town centres from February 2025.

**Why it matters:** Brisbane's reforms represent a critical shift from car-dependent sprawl to transit-oriented infill, demonstrating how mid-tier Australian cities are deploying policy levers—lot size reductions, height bonuses near transit, and streamlined approvals—to unlock missing-middle housing at scale in established suburbs.

## First Approval for 1625-Home Suburb in Sydney's South-West

**Source:** The Urban Developer  
**URL:** https://www.theurbandeveloper.com/articles/macarthur-gardens-north-precinct-r3-approval-nsw  
**Topics:** Australia, Sydney, New South Wales, Housing, Affordable Housing, Public Development

Landcom, New South Wales' public development agency, has secured approval for the first stage of Macarthur Gardens North, a 1,625-home precinct in Sydney's south-west, marking a significant milestone in the state's affordable housing pipeline. The initial approval covers a 130-unit affordable housing building designed by DKO Architects, comprising 64 one-bedroom, 59 two-bedroom, and 7 three-bedroom apartments with a floor space ratio of 2.2:1 yielding 10,753 square metres of gross floor area. All 130 apartments are dedicated to affordable housing. The broader precinct represents one of several major projects Landcom is advancing to address housing supply, including redevelopment of tunnelling dive sites from the WestConnex and metro infrastructure projects. Recent milestones include a $236 million essential worker housing tower approved in Annandale in December 2025, which will kickstart a 580-home precinct, and a $152 million Chatswood dive site project exhibited in March 2026 that will eventually yield up to 1,500 homes for essential workers. Additional Landcom projects in development include a 3,900-home precinct at Glenfield, 15 kilometres from Campbelltown, and a 1,300-home district at Bella Vista, demonstrating the agency's strategic role in delivering affordable and workforce housing across Sydney's growth corridors.

**Why it matters:** Landcom's multi-site affordable housing pipeline illustrates how public land agencies are leveraging transit infrastructure sites and greenfield precincts to deliver scale, with dedicated affordable and essential-worker allocations becoming a standard planning outcome rather than an afterthought.

## Transit-oriented development projects continue to reshape American cities

**Source:** Government Market News  
**URL:** https://govmarketnews.com/transit-oriented-development-projects-us/  
**Topics:** United States, Transit-Oriented Development, Housing, Public Realm, Mixed-Use

A wave of transit-oriented development (TOD) projects totalling over $1.8 billion is reshaping American cities through mixed-use districts anchored by metro stations and transit hubs. In Washington, D.C., officials announced plans to transform underutilized Washington Metropolitan Area Transit Authority property surrounding the Takoma Metro station into a $200 million mixed-use community delivering 430-440 multifamily housing units—at least 15 percent designated as affordable—alongside 15,000-18,000 square feet of ground-floor retail connecting to the existing Takoma corridor. In Baltimore, Maryland is preparing a $1.5 billion redevelopment of the State Center site as a transit-oriented mixed-use district near the State Center Metro Subway station, Cultural Center Light Rail station, and within walking distance of Penn Station, making it one of the city's most transit-accessible development sites. Little Rock's Rock Region METRO is overseeing a $20 million project to transform the River Cities Travel Center into a mixed-use development featuring multifamily housing, retail, community spaces, health facilities, and administrative offices designed as a walkable district with improved pedestrian connections. In Honolulu, a $135 million project near Kūwili Station is part of a broader $1.6 billion infrastructure effort covering approximately 555 acres of the Iwilei-Kapālama corridor, with plans for over 1,000 affordable housing units supported by retail, community facilities, new streets connecting to the waterfront, multi-use paths, and bicycle facilities. Design and planning activities for most projects are expected to continue through 2026, with procurement solicitations anticipated in 2027.

**Why it matters:** These cross-country TOD investments demonstrate a convergence on mixed-use, mixed-income districts as the delivery mechanism for housing near transit, with federal funding driving cities to coordinate infrastructure, housing targets, and pedestrian realm design at the precinct scale rather than site-by-site.
