## Federal Budget 2026 ties housing tax reforms to $2 billion infrastructure fund for planning reform

**Source:** The Conversation / CommBank Economic Insights  
**URL:** https://theconversation.com/will-this-budget-really-make-housing-fairer-for-more-australians-its-a-good-start-282367  
**Topics:** Australia, Housing, Policy, Planning Reform, Federal Budget

Australia's 2026–27 Federal Budget introduced sweeping changes to housing policy, pairing contentious tax reforms with a $2 billion Local Infrastructure Fund designed to incentivise state planning reform. From 1 July 2027, negative gearing concessions will be limited to newly built homes only, while the capital gains tax discount will shift from a flat 50 per cent to an inflation-indexed model, though newly built residential properties may retain preferential treatment. Existing negatively geared investments are grandfathered, but CGT changes apply to all gains realised from 2027 onward. Treasury estimates the tax changes will reduce construction by 35,000 homes over the next decade, but the government argues the $2 billion infrastructure fund—which pays states to loosen restrictive planning laws and boost construction productivity—will more than offset this. Grattan Institute analysis suggests planning reforms could unlock more than 60,000 additional homes annually if states embrace well-located upzoning. CommBank economists forecast dwelling price growth will slow to 3 per cent in 2026 (down from an earlier 5 per cent estimate), driven primarily by three cash rate hikes this year rather than the policy changes themselves. The Budget also includes $45 million for single-touch federal-state approvals and commits to identifying surplus Commonwealth land—including 67 military sites—for housing. The measures are part of the National Housing Accord's 1.2 million homes target, which the National Housing Supply and Affordability Council now projects will be met in June 2030, one year late.

**Why it matters:** This marks a major federal intervention linking tax incentives directly to state planning reform, with implications for how urban designers and councils negotiate density, infrastructure funding, and project feasibility in growth corridors.

## Above the tracks: China's unique model for transit-oriented development

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/features/137522-above-tracks-chinas-unique-model-transit-oriented-development  
**Topics:** Transit-Oriented Development, China, Housing, Infrastructure, Urban Regeneration

Metro maintenance depots—typically occupying 20 to 30 hectares of valuable urban land—are among the most underutilised assets in transit-rich cities, argues Anderson Chider Hsieh, an urban designer and architect based in China who has led multiple transit-oriented developments built directly above active rail facilities. Published May 8, 2026, the essay describes how Chinese cities over the past two decades have systematically built housing, schools, parks, offices and retail atop operating metro depots, transforming single-purpose industrial sites into vertically integrated mixed-use districts. Hsieh, Architecture & Design Lead for China at Surbana Jurong Group and former Vice President at AECOM China, positions the model as applicable beyond China's institutional context: "Most cities don't realise what they already own." Examples include Yangliu Jun in Hangzhou, where new neighbourhoods sit above operational train-cleaning and storage facilities. The approach requires careful structural design to accommodate the depot below while meeting urban design objectives above, but offers a pathway for cities facing pressure to add housing and jobs without endless outward expansion. Hsieh concludes that with the right design, governance and patience, "the depot at the end of your metro line is not a dead asset. It may be the most undervalued piece of land your city already owns." The model contrasts with North American practice, where depots remain fenced-off, single-use sites disconnected from surrounding neighbourhoods.

**Why it matters:** As Australian cities grapple with transit-oriented development around new metro lines in Sydney, Melbourne and Brisbane, air-rights development over operational rail infrastructure offers a tested model for intensifying land use without requiring greenfield expansion.

## Portland publishes Design Review Reform Report with eight concepts to speed development

**Source:** City of Portland  
**URL:** https://www.portland.gov/bps/planning/news/2026/5/14/city-portland-publishes-design-review-reform-report-outlining-options  
**Topics:** Portland, Zoning, Design Review, Planning Reform, Urban Design

Portland's Bureau of Planning and Sustainability released a Design Review Reform Report on May 14, 2026, outlining eight potential regulatory reform concepts intended to streamline development in the city's Design and Historic Resource overlay zones. The report, prepared in response to a December 2025 City Council resolution sponsored by Councillors Zimmerman and Morillo, responds to concern that Portland's 50-year-old design review framework—while ensuring new development contributes to the public realm and neighbourhood context—has become a barrier to project feasibility in the current economic climate. The report presents a range of permanent and temporary options rather than a single recommendation, informed by analysis of recent development activity, consultation with the Design Commission, Historic Landmarks Commission and Development Review Advisory Committee, plus targeted input from developers. Portland's two design overlay zones apply in specific geographies, particularly designated centres where significant development is expected to occur. The reforms build on recent efforts including the 2021 Design Overlay Zone Amendments (DOZA) project, which clarified development allowances and streamlined aspects of the review process. Next steps involve prioritising the concepts; additional public engagement and a legislative land-use process—including public hearings and a City Council vote—would be required before implementation. The report acknowledges tension between modernising regulations to support construction feasibility and maintaining Comprehensive Plan objectives around design quality.

**Why it matters:** Portland's explicit acknowledgment that design review can impede development offers a model for Australian cities—particularly Melbourne and Sydney—where heritage and design overlays are increasingly scrutinised for their role in housing supply constraints.
