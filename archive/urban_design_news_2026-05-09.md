## Belgian 'urban village' delivers social housing with style

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/05/137504-belgian-urban-village-delivers-social-housing-style  
**Topics:** Housing, Public Realm, Affordability, Design Code, Belgium

Heulebrug, a 25-year urban development project in Knokke-Heist, Belgium, achieved 80 percent build-out with 851 living spaces combining market-rate, income-restricted, and publicly-owned social housing. Former mayor Leopold Lippens championed vernacular Flemish architecture, and the design team developed a 'simple yet precise one-page code of urban and architectural standards' governing roofs, windows, doors, and exterior walls. The development's northern and western edges integrate seamlessly with existing urban fabric, while southern and eastern edges face a greenbelt with a trail and right-of-way preserving views of open space described as a 'second coastline.' The first phase (2002) delivered 185 building plots, 26 social units, and 124 private apartments; the second phase (2017) allocated plots to young families with a 20-year resale ban to discourage speculation. The success has prompted more ambitious affordable housing policy for the entire city, with engineer-architect Mathias Delrue noting it demonstrates that municipally-sponsored development can transcend conventional 'housing project' perception.

**Why it matters:** This demonstrates that affordable housing and design excellence are not mutually exclusive—a vital lesson for Australian planners grappling with supply-affordability trade-offs. The one-page design code model offers a scalable, community-rooted alternative to prescriptive zoning.

## After Corbusier: How Southeast Asia Turned the Satellite City Into a Transit Megaproject

**Source:** ArchDaily  
**URL:** https://www.archdaily.com/1041232/after-corbusier-how-southeast-asia-turned-the-satellite-city-into-a-transit-megaproject  
**Topics:** Transit-Oriented Development, Urban Form, Modernism, Southeast Asia, Housing Density

In Southeast Asia, modernist planning principles of rationalized, zoned, and infrastructural urbanism have been adapted under pressures of rapid urbanization, land scarcity, climate, and the need to house large populations quickly, resulting in a persistent 'apparatus' where modernism's afterlife is found not in the tower itself but in the city-making system enabling towers and supporting podiums, malls, and stations to proliferate. The 'satellite city' typology has been repeatedly retooled as new towns, mass housing districts, and more recently as transit-oriented development and rail-linked megaprojects. Transit-oriented development is widely adopted, but carries a risk: as station nodes intensify, urban life can become too concentrated, too internalized, and too controlled—reproducing superblocks and reducing the street's role as civic commons. The essay argues for reading Southeast Asian architectural history through the 'operating system' rather than icons—examining mechanisms that structure daily life at scale regarding where people live, how they move, where they gather, and what counts as 'public.'

**Why it matters:** This critical reframing of modernism's persistence in rapid-growth Asian cities offers crucial perspective for Australian planners designing TOD: the apparatus (density, mixed-use podiums, transit nodes) may be more influential than any single architectural gesture, but careful attention to public life and street networks prevents suburban repetition.

## What's on in May 2026

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/whats-on-in-may-2026/  
**Topics:** Australia, Melbourne, Design, Heritage, Public Realm

Melbourne Design Week returns 14–24 May for an 11-day program of talks, tours, exhibitions, launches, installations and workshops held across Melbourne. This year's program sees Japanese industrial designer Shunji Yamanaka delivering a keynote at the National Communication Museum, Alison Page presenting 25 years of practice at Melbourne School of Design foregrounding Blak design methodologies, and US architect Tom Kundig speaking on residential, commercial and cultural sectors. The 2026 MDW program celebrates Australian-made design by Danielle Brustman, Studio Shields, Dutoit, Thomas Maxam, Dean Norton and Tom Fereday, including an exhibition of the Design the World You Want International Student Ideas Competition at RMIT's Design Hub building and a conversation around naturalistic planting and restoring biodiversity to urban spaces. Beyond Melbourne Design Week, the heritage-listed White Bay Power Station in Sydney—recently restored and reconstructed by heritage architecture firm Design 5 and FDC Construction and Fitout—will open for tours between 18 April and 16 May as part of the broader Australian Heritage Festival celebrations. The programming reflects a strategic emphasis on design discourse, cultural heritage activation, and placemaking conversations that shape how Australian cities function as lived environments.

**Why it matters:** Major design festivals and public heritage activations are forums where urban designers engage with policy-makers and developers on liveability and adaptive reuse. Design Week's focus on sustainable placemaking, Indigenous methodologies, and international practice influence future precinct design and public realm thinking across Australian cities.
