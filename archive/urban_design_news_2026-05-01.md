## Heritage reform in NSW targets adaptive reuse in push to support housing supply

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/Heritage-reform-in-NSW-targets-adaptive-reuse-in-push-to-support-housing-supply/  
**Topics:** NSW, Heritage, Housing, Adaptive Reuse, Policy

The New South Wales government has launched its first comprehensive heritage strategy with a five-year implementation timeline, fundamentally reframing heritage policy as a housing solution rather than an obstacle to development. Heritage Minister Penny Sharpe announced that the strategy will streamline approvals for adaptive reuse of heritage buildings into housing and other uses, while simultaneously introducing a new Heritage Act with harsher penalties for owners who allow heritage properties to deteriorate. Built on two years of consultation with over 1,750 stakeholders—including councils, academics, advocacy groups, property consultants, and government agencies—the strategy centres on three objectives: better reflecting NSW's diverse heritage (including new intangible heritage programs and Aboriginal truth-telling partnerships), simplifying the complex heritage approval system, and providing enhanced support to owners and custodians responsible for managing heritage sites. The strategy also promises new guidance on climate change preparedness for heritage assets and investigates long-term funding models to enable proactive heritage management. The reform positions heritage conservation and activation as complementary to the state's housing commitments, explicitly encouraging greater adaptive reuse of existing heritage building stock to deliver new residential supply without demolition.

**Why it matters:** This policy shift demonstrates how progressive jurisdictions are reconciling heritage protection with housing crises by making adaptive reuse the default pathway—a model Melbourne and Victoria may look to as they balance similar pressures between heritage conservation and densification.

## A Lesson From Urban Icons on Designing Public Space: Get Rid of Traffic

**Source:** CityLab (Bloomberg)  
**URL:** https://www.bloomberg.com/citylab  
**Topics:** Public Space, Europe, Urban Design, Traffic, CityLab

At Bloomberg CityLab 2026 in Madrid (April 27-29), Paris Mayor Anne Hidalgo and architect Norman Foster participated in a mayoral forum that distilled a shared core strategy behind successful urban transformation: removing traffic from public spaces. The summit, which convened over 700 mayors, policymakers, and urban innovators, featured mainstage conversations on AI's impact on urban economies and governance, emerging solutions to housing affordability crises, and mayors' approaches to rebuilding trust amid polarization. The event also launched the Mayors AI Forum in partnership with Johns Hopkins University, aiming to strengthen local government leadership in shaping artificial intelligence development and deployment. Foster featured prominently in a Mayors Innovation Studio session challenging city leaders to rethink design as an underutilized tool of city leadership, exploring how design can be deployed more deliberately to advance priorities from service delivery and economic opportunity to trust, resilience, and quality of life. A parallel session with 150 municipal innovation officers examined open innovation as a governance strategy—how city halls can elevate public problems to communities of potential solution-providers including entrepreneurs, residents, academics, and civic technologists, integrating those ideas into real policy and program delivery.

**Why it matters:** The Madrid summit's convergence on traffic removal as a primary public-space design principle validates the tactical urbanism Melbourne has pursued in recent years and reinforces that global city leaders see streets as spaces for people, not just movement corridors.
