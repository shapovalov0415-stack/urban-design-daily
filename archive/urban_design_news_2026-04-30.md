## Small Lots, Big Impact: NYC Council Proposes Construction Code Reforms to Unlock 35,000 Housing Units

**Source:** NYC Council / Multiple outlets  
**URL:** https://council.nyc.gov/press/2026/04/24/3113/  
**Topics:** New York, Housing, Policy, Infill Development, Construction Codes

On April 24, 2026, New York City Council Speaker Julie Menin announced proposed construction code reforms that could unlock up to 35,000 new housing units on approximately 2,850 small, underutilized lots across all five boroughs. Speaking at the American Institute of Architects' 2026 Honors and Awards Luncheon, Menin unveiled plans to allow as-of-right development of buildings up to 8 stories on small lots between 15 and 27 feet wide, without requiring lengthy zoning approvals. The initiative addresses the city's historic housing crisis, with vacancy at just 1.4 percent—the lowest in more than five decades—and median Manhattan rents at $5,000 per month as of March 2026. Menin also announced creation of the Council Advisory Group on Housing Affordability, co-chaired by Gary LaBarbera of the Building and Construction Trades Council, Barika Williams of the Association for Neighborhood and Housing Development, and James Simmons III of Asland Capital Partners. The advisory group will include architects, engineers, urban planners, finance professionals, and housing advocates tasked with identifying code barriers while maintaining safety standards. The Council is in early discussions with the Mamdani administration and plans to advance both city legislation and advocate for any required state-level changes. Officials estimate the reforms would create jobs, generate tax revenue, and transform often-abandoned parcels into accessible housing, building on the 2024 City of Yes reforms.

**Why it matters:** This represents a pragmatic, code-based approach to housing delivery that bypasses traditional rezoning processes—an increasingly relevant model for other constrained urban markets seeking to activate small sites and achieve infill at scale without political gridlock.

## European Cities Face 'Green Gap': Only 13.5% Meet Urban Forestry Standards, JRC Study Finds

**Source:** EU Joint Research Centre / Nature Communications  
**URL:** https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/jrc-news-and-updates/urban-green-spaces-are-scarce-while-climate-and-wealth-impact-access-2026-04-13_en  
**Topics:** Europe, Climate Adaptation, Public Realm, Equity, Green Infrastructure

A study of 862 European cities published in Nature Communications on April 13, 2026, reveals that only 13.5 percent of the urban population meets the '3-30-300' urban greening principle—advocating for 3 trees visible from every home, 30 percent canopy cover in every neighborhood, and living within 300 meters of high-quality green space. The research, conducted by the EU Joint Research Centre, found 21 percent of residents live in areas meeting none of the three criteria, and highlights a pronounced 'green gap' along geographic and socioeconomic lines. Wealthier central and northern European cities are roughly twice as likely to meet forestry standards compared to lower-income southern and eastern counterparts. Fine-scale analysis using disposable income data at 200-meter to 1-kilometer resolution shows a clear positive correlation between income and access to nature across Europe. The findings arrive as European urban population grew 16 percent between 2010 and 2020 while cities expanded 2.3 percent and green urban areas and tree cover density declined 0.3 percent and 1.6 percent respectively. The research supports the EU's Nature Restoration Regulation, which mandates cities prevent loss of green space and tree cover by 2030 and demonstrate continuous increases thereafter. The JRC recommends prioritizing peri-urban forests for mitigating urban heat island effects and improving air quality, and calls for innovative park siting in densely built historic districts.

**Why it matters:** This data-driven benchmarking of European urban greening exposes structural inequities and establishes a measurable framework for climate adaptation planning, placing pressure on cities to embed social justice into green infrastructure strategies and link urban form with public health outcomes.

## New York City shares new urban forest plan by WXY

**Source:** The Architect's Newspaper  
**URL:** https://www.archpaper.com/2026/04/new-york-city-urban-forest-plan/  
**Topics:** New York, Urban Forestry, Climate Adaptation, Green Infrastructure, Policy

New York City has released a comprehensive urban forest plan developed by WXY architecture and urban design firm, aiming to increase the city's tree canopy coverage from 23 percent to 30 percent by 2040. With approximately 7 million trees as of 2021, the plan represents the city's first coordinated approach to treating the urban forest as integrated infrastructure requiring collaboration across 23 city agencies, private landowners, and neighborhoods. The initiative was led by Mayor Zohran Mamdani's administration through the Mayor's Office of Environmental Justice and Climate (MOEJC) in partnership with NYC Parks, with input from over 8,000 New Yorkers. Chief Climate Officer Louise Yeung and Parks Commissioner Tricia Shimamura played key roles in development. WXY founding principal Claire Weisz emphasized that the urban forest canopy is among the few public systems no single agency can build alone, requiring a unified framework spanning public and private actors. At current growth rates, the city wouldn't reach 30 percent coverage until 2055 or later, making acceleration essential. WXY principal Adam Lubinsky highlighted trees' unique infrastructure value—appreciating rather than depreciating over time, with a 20-year-old street tree providing exponentially more cooling and stormwater absorption than younger specimens. The plan positions New York alongside other U.S. cities pursuing similar strategies, including Boston (led by Stoss Landscape Urbanism), Cambridge (Reed Hilderbrand), Philadelphia (Hinge Collective), and San Francisco.

**Why it matters:** This plan demonstrates how cities are increasingly treating green infrastructure as essential urban systems requiring cross-agency coordination and whole-of-government planning, offering a model for integrated climate adaptation and equity strategies that Melbourne and other cities can learn from.
