## Adelaide Aquatic Centre by JPE Design Studio in collaboration with Warren and Mahoney and Karl Winda Telfer

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/adelaide-aquatic-centre/  
**Topics:** Australia, Adelaide, Public Realm, Heritage, Indigenous Design

Published May 5, 2026, this piece examines the newly completed Adelaide Aquatic Centre, which replaces an existing facility on the city's historic Park Lands—a 760-hectare green belt more than twice the size of New York's Central Park that has remained largely intact since Colonel William Light's 1837 colonial plan. The design team, led by JPE Design Studio with Warren and Mahoney and Cultural Creative Karl Winda Telfer, adopted what Telfer calls 'Country Aligned Design,' sitting on site to uncover sight lines and water stories. The cultural landscape approach softened the architecture's edges, introduced curves, and returned 1,000 square metres to the Park Lands. The broader site includes 15 hectares of landscape design, over 300 new trees, and water-sensitive urban design principles in the reinstated former car park. Height limits range from 3 to 4 stories in mixed-use zones nearby, with some reaching 10-12 stories. Building on Adelaide's Park Lands remains politically fraught; the commission is described as both a professional privilege and a social challenge, given the Park Lands' cultural significance as the city's lungs and urban moat. The mass timber interiors add warmth, while the scheme balances civic infrastructure with a commitment to landscape restoration and First Nations-led placemaking.

**Why it matters:** This project demonstrates how major civic infrastructure can navigate heritage constraints and deliver net-positive landscape outcomes through Country-led design methodologies, offering a replicable model for integrating Indigenous knowledge systems into contested public realm projects.

## Breaking the Single-Family Zoning Bottleneck

**Source:** Politics & Government News  
**URL:** https://politics-government.news-articles.net/content/2026/05/03/breaking-the-single-family-zoning-bottleneck.html  
**Topics:** United States, Zoning Reform, Missing Middle Housing, Policy, ADUs

This May 3, 2026 analysis examines the nationwide movement to legalize 'Missing Middle Housing'—cottage courts, townhouses, and fourplexes that sit between single-family homes and high-rise apartments—as a strategy to address housing supply inelasticity. The piece outlines four key reform mechanisms now deployed across jurisdictions: ending single-family exclusivity by allowing duplexes and triplexes by-right without variance permits; fast-tracking Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) approvals to add rental income and supply; eliminating parking minimums and increasing density around transit hubs under Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) frameworks; and state-level overrides that mandate minimum density requirements to counter local NIMBY sentiment. The political debate pits opponents who cite property values, infrastructure strain, and neighborhood character against YIMBY proponents who argue the social cost of housing instability outweighs aesthetic uniformity. Economically, the article frames density reform as a question of supply elasticity: when housing supply cannot grow regardless of price, prices skyrocket. While reforms alone may not instantly lower prices due to construction costs and labor shortages, they prevent extreme price spikes from artificial scarcity and diversify housing types to serve broader income levels. The piece emphasizes that reforms are spreading from progressive cities to red states, including Georgia's tiny home bills and Georgia HB 1166, which allows secondary units under 400 square feet without local zoning approval.

**Why it matters:** Missing Middle legalization is emerging as the core battleground in North American housing policy, shifting from voluntary local action to state-mandated standards that preempt exclusionary zoning—a trend with direct relevance to Australian infill and medium-density debates.

## FTA Announces $28.5 Million for Transit-Oriented Development Planning

**Source:** Federal Transit Administration  
**URL:** https://www.transit.dot.gov/notices-funding/fy-2026-notice-funding-opportunity-pilot-program-transit-oriented-development  
**Topics:** United States, Transit-Oriented Development, Policy, Federal Funding, Housing

The U.S. Federal Transit Administration announced on May 11, 2026, that $28.5 million is now available through its Fiscal Year 2026 Pilot Program for Transit-Oriented Development Planning. The competitive grant program supports local communities seeking to integrate land use and transportation planning around new fixed-guideway or core-capacity transit capital investments. Applications are due by July 10, 2026. The program aims to enhance economic development, increase transit ridership, facilitate multimodal connectivity, and encourage private sector participation in TOD projects. Priority considerations for funding include safety—how projects create safe routes to schools, childcare, and social services along transit corridors—and innovation, including partnerships with transit agencies and advocates. The program represents a continuation of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act's emphasis on comprehensive planning that links housing, land use, and transportation. There is no minimum or maximum grant award amount, and projects may receive less than requested. The program explicitly encourages planning that supports affordable housing around transit, mixed-use development, and pedestrian and bicycle access to transit hubs. FTA noted the program helps fulfill its mission of building 'Great American Communities' by maintaining or expanding capacity for all travel modes while improving public well-being and climate resilience.

**Why it matters:** Federal TOD planning grants signal ongoing U.S. policy momentum toward integrating land use and transit investment, offering a model for coordinated intergovernmental funding that Australian practitioners can benchmark as states and territories grapple with similar coordination challenges around metro and light-rail projects.
