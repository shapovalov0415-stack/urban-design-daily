## Planning reform will make or break Australia's housing ambition

**Source:** Housing Industry Association  
**URL:** https://hia.com.au/our-industry/housing/in-focus/2026/04/planning-reform-will-make-or-break-australia-housing-ambition  
**Topics:** Australia, Planning Reform, Housing, Policy, Zoning

The Housing Industry Association has released its 2026 Planning Blueprint Scorecard, revealing that no Australian jurisdiction scores higher than three out of five in planning reform performance, even as the country falls 67,000 dwellings short of its annual National Housing Accord target. Under the NHA, governments committed to delivering 1.2 million new homes over five years to June 2029, but Australia must now deliver around 260,000 new homes per year nationally to meet that target—up from the original run rate of 280,000 due to accumulated shortfalls. New South Wales emerges as the most ambitious reformer, implementing state-led rezonings, expanded complying development pathways, pre-endorsed design pattern books, and creating a development coordination authority to tackle delays across the housing pipeline. Western Australia and South Australia record the highest overall planning scores for 2026, combining streamlined approval frameworks with consistent design codes and major land-release programs. South Australia's Housing Roadmap is supported by a modern digital planning system and land supply dashboard that demonstrates how cutting red tape and long-term supply planning can work together. Victoria is gaining momentum through deemed-to-comply standards for townhouses and low-rise housing, though these benefits risk being undermined by additional taxes and charges. Queensland records one of the lowest scores despite clear recommendations from the independent Queensland Productivity Commission. The HIA warns that restrictive zoning, outdated density controls, and excessive overlays continue to limit housing choice, particularly in well-located urban areas—in some capital cities, up to 80 percent of residential land remains locked into low-density zoning.

**Why it matters:** Planning reform is now understood as the system-defining constraint on housing delivery across Australia, determining not just approval speed but fundamental questions of land suitability, permitted yield, and design assessment—making it the critical battleground for addressing the national housing crisis.

## San Diego wants to limit upzoning law to just 16% of transit-adjacent parcels

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/05/137502-san-diego-wants-limit-upzoning-law-just-16-transit-adjacent-parcels  
**Topics:** United States, San Diego, Transit-Oriented Development, Zoning Reform, Housing Supply

San Diego is seeking delays and exemptions from California's new state housing law SB 79, which requires high-rise development near transit stops. The proposal would limit the law's application to just 16 percent of transit-adjacent parcels in the city. The city council is scheduled to take up the proposal on Thursday, May 8, 2026, and their decision would require approval by the state Department of Housing and Community Development. The story reflects broader tensions in California as municipalities grapple with state-mandated transit-oriented development requirements that override local zoning. The San Diego Union-Tribune reports that the city's approach represents one of the more aggressive attempts by a major California city to carve out exemptions from the statewide TOD mandate. The conflict highlights a recurring pattern where transit agencies and cities seek relief from housing laws despite the potential for increased ridership that comes from placing more housing near transit stations. The outcome could set a precedent for how other California cities negotiate with the state over implementation of TOD density mandates, particularly in jurisdictions where local political opposition to upzoning remains strong. SB 79 is part of California's broader strategy to address the housing crisis by concentrating new development near high-quality transit infrastructure.

**Why it matters:** The tension between state-mandated TOD and local implementation reveals how even progressive jurisdictions struggle to operationalise density near transit, offering lessons for Melbourne and other Australian cities now pursuing their own station-area upzoning reforms under state-led frameworks.

## Urban Trees Cool Cities More Than Expected, Not Enough

**Source:** Mirage News  
**URL:** https://www.miragenews.com/urban-trees-cool-cities-more-than-expected-not-1668791/  
**Topics:** Climate Adaptation, Urban Greening, Public Health, Urban Heat, Urban Design

New research published two days ago reveals that urban trees reduce city heat more effectively than previously understood, but warns they cannot be the sole solution to rising urban temperatures. Cities and towns are typically 1–3°C hotter than surrounding countryside because asphalt, concrete, and brick absorb and radiate solar heat. While authorities around the world have promoted tree planting as a simple solution to urban heat islands, the research finds that current urban tree canopy cover typically addresses only a small fraction of excess heat. With ambitious planting programs, tree coverage could rise to around 20 percent of the heat mitigation needed, but a large majority of the extra heat will remain unaddressed. The study emphasises that trees must be seen as part of a broader urban design strategy rather than the whole answer. Clever urban design can reduce heat through reflective materials, increasing green spaces, and improving airflow between buildings. The research also highlights equity concerns: the people who need free cooling from trees the most are often the least likely to receive it, as lower-income neighborhoods typically have less tree cover than wealthier areas. Trees offer co-benefits including cleaner air and better mental health, making them valuable urban infrastructure even if their cooling effect is limited. The findings arrive as cities worldwide confront more frequent and intense heatwaves driven by climate change.

**Why it matters:** Urban designers must abandon tree planting as a singular heat-mitigation strategy and instead embed canopy within comprehensive urban form interventions—including materials, building setbacks, and wind corridors—that address the majority of urban heat island effects trees alone cannot solve.
