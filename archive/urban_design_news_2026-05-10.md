## Missoula's Zoning Reform Raises the Bar for Small Cities

**Source:** Sightline Institute  
**URL:** https://www.sightline.org/2026/04/28/missoulas-zoning-reform-raises-the-bar-for-small-cities/  
**Topics:** Missoula, Montana, Zoning Reform, Housing, Policy

Missoula, Montana has completed a comprehensive zoning overhaul driven by the state's 2023 Montana Land Use and Planning Act (MLUPA), meeting the May 2026 deadline set by the legislation. The reforms mark a dramatic shift for the city, which had spent years resisting change—a 2012 ADU proposal passed only after contentious debate and produced just a handful of units due to attached restrictions. Under MLUPA, Montana's largest cities were required to relax zoning regulations to enable infill homebuilding and adopt faster project review processes. Missoula's new code now permits ADUs, garage conversions, apartment buildings, small-scale multiplexes, and townhomes by right on most blocks, with no density maximums. Mixed-use districts have no unit-count or floor-area-ratio caps, with height limits ranging from 4 to 12 stories. Nearly all residential neighborhoods now allow three-story buildings, with four stories permitted in central areas. Pushback emerged from the Rattlesnake neighborhood near downtown, where residents cited concerns about solar-panel shading and urban heat islands, prompting small zone adjustments. However, organized advocacy from groups like ProHousing Missoula ensured city leaders maintained the ambitious reforms. The overhaul is notable not just for its innovation but for its replicability, offering a template for similarly sized cities nationwide facing state-mandated housing reform.

**Why it matters:** This case demonstrates how state-level housing mandates can unlock locally stalled zoning reform, particularly in smaller cities where political inertia has historically blocked incremental change. The comprehensiveness of Missoula's reforms—eliminating density caps and enabling by-right development—offers a scalable model for jurisdictions grappling with affordability crises.

## Jersey City Approves 55-Story Tower With Whole Foods Market

**Source:** Jersey Digs  
**URL:** https://jerseydigs.com/jersey-city-55-story-tower-whole-foods-journal-square/  
**Topics:** United States, New Jersey, TOD, Housing, Transit

The Jersey City Planning Board approved an 840-unit mixed-use residential tower in the Journal Square neighborhood on April 28, 2026, marking a significant transit-oriented development adjacent to the Journal Square Transportation Center. The 55-story project will include 84 affordable housing units and feature a ground-floor Whole Foods Market, with zero car parking spaces—a bold move that leverages the site's exceptional transit connectivity via NJ TRANSIT and PATH rail services. The development represents a new wave of car-free, high-density housing near major transit hubs in New Jersey, aligning with state and regional goals to concentrate residential growth around rail stations. Journal Square has been identified as a key growth node in Hudson County's planning framework, with the intersection of multiple transit lines making it one of the most accessible locations outside Manhattan. The approval follows New Jersey Governor Mikie Sherrill's recent executive order aimed at accelerating housing production across the state, including directives for NJ TRANSIT to identify underutilized agency-owned properties suitable for transit-oriented development. The project's mix of market-rate and affordable units, combined with its rejection of automobile parking, signals a planning approach that prioritizes walkability, transit ridership, and urban density over car accommodation—a strategy increasingly adopted by cities seeking to address housing shortages while reducing transportation emissions.

**Why it matters:** This project exemplifies the emerging generation of car-free TOD in North America, demonstrating how jurisdictions can leverage transit infrastructure to unlock dense, mixed-income housing while challenging automobile-centric development norms—a model Melbourne and other cities are actively exploring.
