## Finland Opens World's Longest Car-Free Bridge

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/05/  
**Topics:** Transit, Mobility, Sustainable Urban Design, Europe

Finland has completed and opened the world's longest car-free bridge, a significant infrastructure milestone for sustainable mobility in Europe. The bridge represents a bold commitment to modal shift away from private vehicle dependency and toward pedestrian, cycling, and public transit movement patterns. This project reflects broader European urbanism trends prioritizing multimodal networks and car-free public realms, positioning walking and cycling infrastructure as essential urban design elements rather than secondary accommodations. The scale and prominence of the bridge—being the longest of its type globally—signals shifting political will and public support for car-free urban mobility in a Scandinavian context. The completion demonstrates technical feasibility of large-scale, long-span pedestrian and cycling infrastructure while maintaining safety, accessibility, and connectivity standards. European cities are increasingly adopting car-free design strategies as part of climate resilience and livability agendas, and Finland's bridge joins other examples such as urban car-free zones, pedestrianized streets, and bike-priority networks reshaping city centers.

**Why it matters:** This represents a tangible infrastructure model for transit-oriented and car-light urban design that practitioners globally—especially in Australian cities considering similar modal shift—can reference when advocating for pedestrian and cycling priority in public realm investment.

## Australia falling well behind national housing target

**Source:** Canberra CityNews  
**URL:** https://citynews.com.au/2026/australia-falling-well-behind-national-housing-target/  
**Topics:** Australia, Housing, Planning Policy, Zoning, National Policy

The housing accord has an ambitious target of 1.2 million new homes across Australia by June 2029. No state or territory is on track to meet its housing accord targets, with only 219,000 new homes built in the first five quarters of the agreement, well shy of the required run rate of 280,000 new homes, as revealed by the National Housing Supply and Affordability Council in its first quarterly report. The accord set an ambitious target of 1.2 million new homes across Australia over the five years to June 2029, but the national target is only on track to be completed by June 2030, with Victoria, Western Australia and the ACT the best performing jurisdictions, only set to complete their share of the target by September 2029. Measures such as relaxing zoning for middle density housing and removing third-party appeals for public housing appeared to be paying dividends for the ACT, with 117 per cent more dwellings approved in the year to January compared to the 12 months prior. The outlook for Victoria was less promising, with building approvals falling one per cent over the year to January, despite efforts to boost density in priority areas, streamlining approvals and a vacant land tax to unlock more space for development. The report underscores that planning reform, zoning reform, and density allowances are essential but insufficient on their own; state and territory governments must accelerate implementation and address systemic approval delays and infrastructure constraints.

**Why it matters:** For Australian urban designers and planners, this national tracking data reveals that zoning and density reforms announced across jurisdictions are not translating at the pace needed to address the housing crisis. The ACT's success with middle-density zoning and streamlined approvals for public housing provides a policy template, while Victoria's approval slowdown despite reforms signals that planning code change alone requires parallel investment in assessment capacity and infrastructure coordination.
