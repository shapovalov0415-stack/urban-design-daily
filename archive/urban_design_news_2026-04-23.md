## New 10-year plan for Perth city unveiled

**Source:** ArchitectureAU  
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/new-10-year-plan-for-perth-city-unveiled/  
**Topics:** Australia, Public Space, Streetscape, Placemaking

The City of Perth has endorsed Shaping Perth City Centre – Towards 2036 and Beyond, a ten-year strategic framework developed with Danish urban strategy firm Gehl Architects, extending what is now a 30-year collaboration between the two. The plan sets five strategic goals and 20 implementation directions and identifies five catalytic Key Moves intended to act as the spine of the next decade of investment: North-South Connectors, the Buneenboro Loop, Living Streets, The Malls Evolution, and East-West Transit Corridors. The Buneenboro Loop, named with the Noongar term for the Swan River, is a continuous waterfront walking and cycling route stitching Derbarl Yerrigan, Kaarta Gar-up (Kings Park), the Perth Convention and Exhibition Centre, Elizabeth Quay and Langley Park into a single legible public realm. William Street is earmarked to become a living street – a green, walkable, bike-friendly spine with reduced through-traffic, dining edges and activated ground floors – while Barrack Street is reframed as a cultural axis. A new People's Walk would connect Central Perth Station to Forrest Place, Carillon City and Elizabeth Quay, knitting the station forecourt to the river. The framework was developed with more than 80 stakeholders from private industry and government, and is pegged to a demographic target of 55,000 residents in the city centre by 2036 and up to 90,000 by 2050 – roughly doubling to tripling the current population. The Malls Evolution direction revisits Murray and Hay Street Malls as primary retail anchors needing curated programming, evening activation and a resilient tenancy mix to survive shifts in office attendance and online retail.

**Why it matters for an urban designer:** Indigenous-named waterfront loop plus a William Street 'living street' is a template Melbourne could port onto Elizabeth Street or the Yarra edge.

## NYC announces new 'pied-a-terre tax' on second homes

**Source:** Planetizen  
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/04/137381-nyc-announces-new-pied-terre-tax-second-homes  
**Topics:** US, Housing, Policy, Zoning

Mayor Zohran Mamdani and Governor Kathy Hochul announced on 15 April a joint proposal for New York's first pied-a-terre tax, an annual surcharge on one-to-three family homes, condominiums and co-ops valued above US$5 million when the owner's primary residence sits outside New York City. The city estimates roughly 13,000 properties would be affected and projects the tax would raise at least US$500 million a year – revenue the mayor argues will help close the city's spending deficit and fund housing, transit and public-service priorities. Polling cited by the mayor's office put support among New Yorkers at 93 percent, while the real estate lobby has pushed back hard, warning the surcharge would depress property values, deter investment and cut construction jobs. The measure still needs to pass the New York State Legislature, where similar proposals have stalled before. For urban designers, the proposal matters less as a tax story than as a signal about the city's posture toward housing that sits empty most of the year. Shadow-city anxieties – lights-off condo towers along 57th Street's Billionaires' Row – have shaped debate for over a decade, but this is the first time a sitting mayor and governor have formally aligned behind pricing the external cost of that absenteeism. The policy does not, by itself, return those units to resident use, but it reframes ultra-luxury second homes as a drain on the tax base rather than a civic trophy, and makes land-value capture at the top of the market politically live again.

**Why it matters for an urban designer:** Live test of whether taxing vacant luxury housing nudges supply – a question Melbourne also faces with its vacant residential land tax and absentee-owner surcharge.

## Reclaiming the Street: Alejandra Ferrera on Architecture and Urban Life in Honduras

**Source:** ArchDaily  
**URL:** https://www.archdaily.com/1039699/reclaiming-the-street-alejandra-ferrera-on-architecture-and-urban-life-in-honduras  
**Topics:** Public Space, Placemaking, Streetscape, Heritage

Honduran architect Alejandra Ferrera argues in a long ArchDaily interview that decades of security-driven design have hollowed out the public realm in Tegucigalpa and other Honduran cities, reducing the street to a transit void rather than a place of social encounter. Raised in Danli and trained across Brazil, the Netherlands and Australia, Ferrera brings fifteen years of comparative practice to the diagnosis. The shift she traces is familiar across much of Latin America: high perimeter walls, gated compounds, privatised amenities, and a retreat of middle-class domestic life from the sidewalk inward. Ferrera accepts that fear was a reasonable response to real crime in the 1990s and 2000s, but argues the city never rebuilt the connective tissue that walls replaced – porous ground floors, shaded seating, legible public frontages, small commerce along the kerb. The consequence, she argues, is a wounded national identity that instinctively looks to Miami or Madrid for design references and dismisses the ordinary fabric of Honduran barrios as backdrop rather than raw material. Her own practice sits in explicit opposition: projects that re-privilege the street, reuse local timber and masonry, and treat vernacular typologies as design intelligence rather than nostalgia. Ferrera points to heritage-led work in Comayagua and incremental public-space repairs in Tegucigalpa as evidence the pattern can be reversed, and calls on younger Honduran practices to stop treating security and good public space as a tradeoff. The piece lands as several Latin American cities openly reposition public safety as an urban-design outcome rather than a security-forces one.

**Why it matters for an urban designer:** Hard counter to defensive design: reminds designers that active frontage and eyes-on-the-street are a safety strategy, not just amenity.
