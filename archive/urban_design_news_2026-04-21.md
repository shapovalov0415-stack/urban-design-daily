# Urban Design News — 2026-04-21

## 1. Winners revealed: Australian Urban Design Awards 2026
**Source:** ArchitectureAU
**URL:** https://architectureau.com/articles/winners-revealed-2026-australian-urban-design-awards/
**Topics:** Melbourne, Australia, Public Space, Heritage

The 2026 Australian Urban Design Awards were unveiled in Canberra, recognising twelve winners across nine projects and three individuals selected from a 27-project shortlist. The Built Outcomes category — the core recognition for realised civic work — delivered three winners that collectively map the current range of Australian urban design practice. Melbourne's St Kilda Pier Redevelopment, delivered by Jackson Clements Burrows Architects with Site Office Landscape Architecture and AW Maritime, was described by the jury as a major piece of civic engineering that is both resilient and whimsical, acknowledging a project that rebuilt a climate-exposed bayside pier as a continuous piece of public infrastructure rather than a nostalgic restoration. Alongside it, Balam Balam Place in Brunswick by Kennedy Nolan with Open Work and Finding Infinity was recognised as a socially inclusive civic precinct developed through a sensitive approach to the renewal of existing heritage fabric — heritage-led urbanism treated as live infrastructure rather than museum practice. The third Built Outcomes winner, Campbelltown Station Commuter Carpark in Sydney by Hill Thalis Architecture and Urban Projects, reconceived a purely utilitarian building type as enduring civic infrastructure animated by colour and planting, signalling the jury's interest in dignifying the everyday pieces of the transit network rather than reserving design attention for marquee stations. Read together, the three winners suggest the awards are rewarding projects that reframe infrastructure — a pier, a heritage precinct, a park-and-ride — as long-life civic assets with visible landscape and social programming.

**Why it matters for an urban designer:** Three infrastructure types — pier, heritage precinct, commuter carpark — reframed as civic assets. Design discipline now applies to the everyday pieces too.

## 2. Chicago eliminates parking requirements near transit
**Source:** Planetizen
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/04/137368-chicago-eliminates-parking-requirements-near-transit
**Topics:** US, Zoning, Transit, Housing

Chicago has fully eliminated minimum parking requirements for new housing within walking distance of Chicago Transit Authority rail stations, Metra stations and high-frequency bus corridors, completing a two-stage reform that now aligns the city with the incoming Illinois People Over Parking Act. The local ordinance builds on the 2022 Connected Communities Ordinance, which halved parking minimums in transit-served areas and allowed zero parking for assisted or affordable projects, and a 2025 amendment that let developments outside downtown provide zero parking without administrative adjustment. The latest change is the clean version: no minimum, no case-by-case ask. Paired with the reform, the city expanded by-right accessory dwelling units to all multifamily residential districts, meaning zoning now pulls in the same direction rather than creating a workaround. The state law, the People Over Parking Act, takes effect 1 June 2026 and prohibits any Illinois municipality from imposing minimum automobile parking on projects within half a mile of a transit hub or an eighth of a mile of a transit corridor, with hotels and motels carved out. Researchers cited in the coverage estimate surface parking spaces add more than 4,000 dollars per unit to construction costs and structured spaces more than 37,000 dollars, costs that have historically flowed through to rent — roughly a 17 percent premium in some studies. For practitioners, the reform consolidates a template: eliminate minimums, pair with ADU liberalisation, and let the housing rather than the car define block-scale form.

**Why it matters for an urban designer:** Illinois becomes the fourth US state to end transit-area parking minimums. The template — end minimums, pair with ADU liberalisation — is ready to adapt.

## 3. Atlanta's Beltline hits 79% of its affordable housing goal
**Source:** Planetizen
**URL:** https://www.planetizen.com/news/2026/04/137289-atlantas-beltline-hits-79-its-affordable-housing-goal
**Topics:** US, Housing, Placemaking, Policy

Atlanta Beltline, Inc. has announced it is at 79 percent of its 2030 affordable housing target, reporting 4,425 affordable or preserved units delivered inside the Beltline Tax Allocation District against a goal of 5,600 units. The progress reframes a project that was flagged in earlier years as falling behind: the agency delivered 299 units in 2025 and is projecting another 300 to 500 per year through the late 2020s, with a live pipeline of roughly 500 units currently in active development. Three projects carry the near-term weight. 579 Garson Drive, expected to close in the second quarter of 2026, will deliver 130 permanently affordable homes alongside 8,000 square feet of commercial space. 350 Chappell Road, a 6.3-acre site targeting 218 affordable units at 30 to 80 percent area median income, is scheduled to close this year. Waterworks opened in December 2025 with 100 studio apartments structured as rapid housing for formerly unhoused residents. The Beltline is also running a displacement prevention program that subsidises property-tax increases for long-time residents through 2030 — an explicit acknowledgement that the greenway has generated value uplift that falls on the people it was meant to serve. A new Affordable Housing Dashboard launched in January 2026 maps each delivered development and its affordability band, making the 4,425-unit figure independently auditable. For designers, the lesson is that a linear greenway functions as land policy: the trail programs the zone of uplift, and the housing target plus dashboard are how that uplift is captured.

**Why it matters for an urban designer:** The trail creates the uplift; the affordable housing target and dashboard decide who keeps it. A loop Melbourne's greenway corridors will eventually need.
