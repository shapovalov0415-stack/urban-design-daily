## Apartment Sector Drags Down National Home Approvals

**Source:** The Urban Developer  
**URL:** https://www.theurbandeveloper.com/articles/abs-home-approval-national-march-2026  
**Topics:** Australia, Housing, Policy, Supply, Approvals

National dwelling approvals in Australia fell 10.5 percent in March 2026 to 17,300 homes, according to seasonally adjusted Australian Bureau of Statistics data released May 5. The decline was driven by a 26 percent drop in private homes excluding houses—primarily apartments—after February's 101.1 percent surge. However, private sector house approvals rose 0.9 percent to 10,194 homes, reaching the highest level since November 2021 and sitting 12 percent higher year-on-year. New South Wales recorded the largest rise in house approvals, up 9.5 percent to the highest level since August 2022, while Western Australia saw an 8.6 percent fall. Urban Taskforce Australia chief executive Tom Forrest noted that annualised results now touch 200,000 dwellings per year, still well below the National Housing Accord target of 240,000 completed homes annually. Master Builders Australia chief economist Shane Garrett warned that higher interest rates and escalating Middle East conflict pressures on construction costs will further constrain demand into 2027. The data underscores the challenge of delivering housing supply at scale: while house approvals are rebounding from subdued pandemic-era levels, medium- and high-density project feasibility remains constrained by elevated costs, tight contracting capacity and prolonged approval timeframes that squeeze margins through escalation and holding costs.

**Why it matters:** The divergence between strong detached-house approval growth and collapsing apartment approvals signals that Australia's Housing Accord targets will remain out of reach without policy interventions to make medium-density projects financially viable—a critical consideration for urban designers advocating transit-oriented and infill development.

## USDOT Proposes New TIFIA, RRIF Loan Sizing Methodology for TOD Projects

**Source:** The Transit Friendly Planning Newsletter (NJ TOD)  
**URL:** https://www.njtod.org/news-may-1-2026/  
**Topics:** Transit-Oriented Development, Federal Policy, United States, Infrastructure Finance, TOD

The US Department of Transportation proposed a new methodology on April 27, 2026 for sizing TIFIA and RRIF loans for transit-oriented development projects based on demonstrated transit value rather than traditional project finance metrics. According to a Nixon Peabody analysis, the approach would prioritize projects that measurably increase transit ridership and revenue through infrastructure improvements or revenue-generating joint developments such as ground leases or profit-sharing arrangements. This represents a significant policy shift: rather than evaluating TOD projects primarily on real estate feasibility, USDOT will now assess the degree to which a development enhances the performance and financial sustainability of the underlying transit system. The new framework aims to unlock federal loan capacity for mixed-use developments at station areas that have struggled to secure conventional financing due to the difficulty of underwriting the transit-adjacent premium. Projects demonstrating higher transit value—measured by factors such as increased farebox revenue, reduced operating subsidies, or improved network connectivity—would qualify for larger loan amounts and potentially more favorable terms. The proposal arrives as cities nationwide grapple with post-pandemic transit ridership recovery and seek to monetize publicly owned land near stations to cross-subsidize service improvements.

**Why it matters:** By directly linking federal financing to measurable transit system benefits, this policy recalibrates TOD incentives away from speculative real estate toward functionally integrated station-area development, offering urban designers a clearer mandate and financing path for projects that genuinely support ridership and network performance.

## Housing and conservation groups propose guardrails for housing on public lands

**Source:** KSUT Public Radio / Mountain West News Bureau  
**URL:** https://www.ksut.org/2026-04-30/housing-and-conservation-groups-propose-guardrails-for-housing-on-public-lands  
**Topics:** Housing, Policy, United States, Federal Policy, Public Lands

More than 60 conservation and affordable housing organisations have published "Shared Ground," a policy paper pushing back against proposals from members of Congress to sell federal public lands as a solution to the housing crisis. The paper, released on April 30, 2026, argues that housing affordability is "fundamentally a policy and investment challenge—not the result of a simple shortage of land." The groups include the National Housing Law Project, Nevada Housing Justice Alliance, Nevada Wildlife Federation, New Mexico Wild, Southern Utah Wilderness Alliance, Conservation Colorado, Wyoming Wilderness Association and Wyoming Outdoor Council. Noëlle Porter, director of government affairs at the National Housing Law Project, noted affordable housing advocates were caught off guard when several congressional measures were introduced last summer, including proposals from Senator Mark Amodei (R-Nevada) and broader plans from other lawmakers. While those proposals failed, the groups wanted to establish criteria for any future plans. The paper lays out specific guardrails: legally binding affordability requirements, prioritising already-developed sites, excluding land with significant conservation, recreation or cultural value, and ensuring projects fit with local land use plans and involve consultation with Tribes and communities. The report acknowledges bipartisan interest in using federal land for housing—the Biden Administration launched a program to use Forest Service administrative parcels for employee housing—but argues that alternatives like preserving existing affordable housing, building on vacant land within existing neighbourhoods, and zoning reform should be prioritised. Meanwhile, Interior Secretary Doug Burgum has his own plan announced over a year ago in partnership with HUD, but has made no public moves since, with HUD now the lead agency.

**Why it matters:** The paper offers a rare convergence of conservation and housing advocacy groups around a shared framework for evaluating public land proposals, signalling that land-use debates around the housing crisis are shifting from binary opposition to nuanced criteria focused on affordability guarantees, site selection, and community engagement—essential considerations for urban designers working on transit-adjacent or greenfield sites.
